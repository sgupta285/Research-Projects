\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Backtest Sharpe ratios routinely overstate live-trading performance.
Execution-modeling assumptions---fill prices, transaction costs, market
impact---contribute substantially to this inflation, yet most studies
apply na\"{i}ve fill models without quantifying the distortion each
simplification introduces. We present a transparent, event-driven
backtesting engine with causality enforcement and double-entry accounting
invariants, applied to ten liquid US equity ETFs over 2005--2025.
A six-tier execution realism ladder (M0: na\"{i}ve fills through
M5: delayed execution) enables per-tier cost attribution.
Applied to time-series momentum (TSMOM-60), cross-sectional momentum
(CSMOM-60), and a mean-reversion diagnostic (MeanRev-z1), we find:
(i)~TSMOM-60's na\"{i}ve Sharpe of 0.53 collapses to 0.020 under
baseline impact parameters ($\kimp=0.50$, $\rho=0.05$), a $25.7\times$
inflation ratio---sensitivity sweep spans $\sim$$1.3\times$--$25.7\times$
across $\kimp \in [0, 0.50]$, so the magnitude is highly
calibration-dependent;
(ii)~CSMOM-60 is statistically positive pre-cost ($b=20$ CI $[+0.20,
+1.00]$) but sign-flips under M4 (Sharpe $-0.242$, CI spanning zero);
(iii)~MeanRev-z1 sign-flips at M1 (fees alone), diagnosing a fatally
cost-sensitive strategy before capital is committed.
Moving-block bootstrap CIs ($b=20$, $N=1{,}000$) confirm all conclusions.
Lookback sensitivity shows TSMOM-120 achieves M4 Sharpe $+0.276$
(at $\kimp=0.50$), establishing lookback as a primary design lever for
impact-constrained momentum strategies.
The engine and all data snapshots are released as open-source software.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Keywords:} backtesting, execution realism, transaction costs,
market impact, time-series momentum, cross-sectional momentum, Sharpe
ratio inference, block bootstrap, event-driven simulation.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{JEL:} G12, G14, G23, C63.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

A practitioner who codes a daily momentum strategy, observes a Sharpe
ratio near 0.5 on five years of ETF data, and allocates capital to it
will commonly find that the strategy earns a fraction of the backtested
performance, or loses money outright. This outcome is predictable
ex ante from first principles: it follows from the combination of
near-zero gross edge per trade, positive turnover, and execution
assumptions that ignore the three primary cost channels of institutional
trading---spread, slippage, and market impact. Yet most academic
backtests continue to apply na\"{i}ve fill assumptions, and most
published factor studies report performance metrics without per-tier
cost attribution that would reveal how sensitive the result is to each
source of execution friction.

The \emph{statistical} sources of backtest inflation have received
substantial academic attention. \citet{harvey2016and} catalogue over
300 published factors and recommend a $t$-statistic hurdle of 3.0 to
account for implicit multiple testing. \citet{bailey2014deflated}
formalise the Deflated Sharpe Ratio, which penalises reported
performance by the effective number of strategies evaluated.
\citet{white2000reality} and \citet{sullivan1999data} develop bootstrap
tests under which most technical trading rule returns vanish.
\citet{harvey2020false} develop a double-bootstrap method for
controlling both Type~I (false discoveries) and Type~II (missed
discoveries) errors in factor and fund selection. \citet{hou2020replicating} find that the
majority of published anomalies fail to replicate under a standardised
methodology. What these contributions share is a focus on
\emph{statistical} inflation in the strategy signal itself.

The present paper addresses the orthogonal dimension:
\emph{execution-modeling inflation}, the performance distortion
introduced when backtests simulate fills under assumptions that bear
little resemblance to institutional trading. Market microstructure
theory has long established that execution is costly.

\paragraph{Permanent vs.\ Temporary Impact.}
\citet{kyle1985continuous} models \emph{permanent} price impact in a
sequential-auction Bayesian-Nash equilibrium: the resulting Kyle lambda
$\lambda = \partial P/\partial Q$ is a constant, yielding linear
permanent impact. This is distinct from the \emph{temporary} concave
impact relationship we implement. The empirical square-root scaling of
temporary impact, $\iota \propto \sqrt{Q/\mathrm{ADV}}$, is a separate
relationship established by \citet{lillo2003master} from cross-market
institutional order-flow data and surveyed by \citet{bouchaud2009theory}.
We implement this square-root temporary-impact law; Kyle's linear
permanent-impact model is not directly applicable to our setting.
\citet{almgren2005direct}, using a large proprietary institutional dataset,
reject the square-root form in favour of a $3/5$ power law
($\Delta P \propto (Q/V)^{0.6}$); we discuss this alternative
in Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity} and Appendix~\ref{app:power_law}.
\citet{gatheral2010no} provides a no-dynamic-arbitrage
theoretical foundation for concave impact functions, supporting both
the square-root and the $3/5$ power-law specifications. Large-scale
empirical studies confirm that many academic anomalies are not
exploitable after realistic transaction costs
\citep{novymarx2016taxonomy, korajczyk2004momentum, patton2020costs,
frazzini2015trading}. Despite this evidence, the infrastructure for
transparent, tier-by-tier cost attribution remains underdeveloped in
academic research. The few open-source backtesting environments either
lack composable cost tiers or lack formal correctness verification.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
This paper makes three contributions.

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Verified open-source engine.} We present a minimal,
    auditable, event-driven backtesting engine with a formal unit-test
    suite verifying strict causality, the double-entry accounting
    identity, fill-quantity bounds, and cost monotonicity. The full
    source and a frozen data snapshot are publicly available at:\\
    \url{https://github.com/sgupta285/Research-Projects/tree/main/backtest-engine-paper}.

  \item \textbf{Six-level execution realism ladder.} We decompose
    execution costs into six composable, independently activatable
    tiers (M0--M5), from na\"{i}ve fills to delayed square-root-impact
    execution, with a single authoritative parameter table and
    block-bootstrap CIs at $b \in \{10, 20, 30\}$.

  \item \textbf{Three-strategy attribution study spanning the
    viability spectrum.} TSMOM-60 (time-series momentum, directional
    signal, CI entirely positive at M0--M1, boundary case at M2
    ($\ell = +0.003$), collapsing to span zero by M3), CSMOM-60 (cross-sectional momentum, relative ranking signal,
    CI positive pre-cost at $b = 20$ but sign-flipping at M4), and
    MeanRev-z1 (cost-sensitivity diagnostic, CI spanning zero pre-cost,
    sign-flip at M1). Together these three
    strategies span the full range from clearly non-viable to arguably
    viable, illustrating the diagnostic power of the execution ladder
    across qualitatively different pre-cost regimes.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Key findings.}
TSMOM-60's Sharpe inflation ratio between M0 and M4 is $25.7\times$
(at $\kimp=0.50$, $\rho=0.05$; $0.526 \to 0.020$), with M4 spanning
zero under $b = 20$.
CSMOM-60's M0 Sharpe of 0.583 has a positive $b = 20$ lower CI bound
($[+0.20, +1.00]$), establishing a genuine pre-cost signal; under M4,
it sign-flips to $-0.242$ with CI spanning zero. MeanRev-z1 sign-flips at M1,
demonstrating that a five-basis-point fee destroys the entire gross
edge when turnover reaches 180--200 round-trips per year.
Cross-source validation against Stooq confirms return correlations
above 0.9999 for all ten instruments. At $J=60$, no active strategy
maintains a CI entirely above zero under realistic costs; the passive
buy-and-hold benchmark does
($b = 20$ CI $[+0.09,\;+0.87]$), establishing it as the
appropriate performance null.

\paragraph{Scope.}
All quantitative findings are specific to this ten-ETF universe,
the 2005--2025 sample, and the parameters in Table~\ref{tab:params}.
No extrapolation to other asset classes or strategy families is
implied. The contribution is methodological: a reusable, verifiable
engine and an attribution framework that makes per-tier cost accounting
accessible without requiring production-scale simulation infrastructure.

The paper is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} situates
the work. Section~\ref{sec:design} describes the engine.
Section~\ref{sec:ladder} specifies the six execution tiers.
Section~\ref{sec:setup} describes the experimental design.
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents results.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} interprets findings.
Section~\ref{sec:limitations} acknowledges limitations.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Statistical Backtest Bias and Multiple Testing}

\citet{white2000reality} and \citet{sullivan1999data} develop
bootstrap-based reality checks showing that most technical trading rule
excess returns vanish under proper multiple-comparison correction.
\citet{hansen2005test} strengthens White's Reality Check with the
Superior Predictive Ability (SPA) test; \citet{romano2005stepwise}
extend this further with stepwise tests that identify \emph{which}
models outperform, not merely whether any model does.
\citet{harvey2016and} catalogue over 300 factors and argue that a
$t$-statistic threshold of 3.0 is required; the conventional 1.96 is
severely inadequate given the implicit testing involved in factor
discovery. \citet{bailey2014deflated} formalise the Deflated Sharpe
Ratio. \citet{harvey2020false} develop a double-bootstrap method for
controlling both Type~I and Type~II errors in factor and fund
selection. \citet{hou2020replicating} find that the majority of
published anomalies fail to replicate under a standardised methodology.
These works address \emph{statistical} inflation in the signal;
the present paper addresses the complementary \emph{execution-modeling}
dimension.

\subsection{Transaction Costs in Strategy Research}

\citet{keim1997transactions} document that institutional round-trip
execution costs routinely exceed 50--100 bps for mid-cap stocks and
vary systematically with order size and instrument liquidity.
\citet{lesmond1999new} construct an implicit transaction-cost measure
from the incidence of zero-return days, providing a parsimonious
cross-sectional cost proxy available without trade-level data.
\citet{frazzini2015trading} (working paper) document, using proprietary
AQR trade data, that institutional costs can be lower than TAQ-based
estimates for large-cap names, but remain material for high-turnover
strategies; \citet{novymarx2016taxonomy} reach a similar practical
conclusion in a broad anomaly-cost setting.
\citet{amihud2002illiquidity} demonstrates that illiquidity is itself
a priced risk factor. \citet{hasbrouck2009trading} provides a
comprehensive survey of transaction cost measurement methodology.

\subsection{Price Impact Models}

Two distinct classes of price impact must be distinguished.
\emph{Permanent impact} refers to the long-run shift in the efficient
price after a trade. \citet{kyle1985continuous} derives a linear
permanent-impact equilibrium ($\partial P/\partial Q = \lambda$,
constant) in a sequential-auction model with Bayesian-Nash strategies.
Kyle's model is not our implementation here---it does not produce a
concave temporary-impact schedule---but it establishes that informed
order flow endogenously moves prices.

\emph{Temporary impact} refers to the short-run execution shortfall
that reverts after the trade. The empirical square-root law of
temporary impact, $\iota \propto \sqrt{Q/\mathrm{ADV}}$, is
established by \citet{lillo2003master} from cross-market institutional
order-flow data and surveyed by \citet{bouchaud2009theory}.
\citet{gatheral2010no} provides a no-dynamic-arbitrage theoretical
justification for concave (sublinear) temporary-impact functions,
supporting the square-root specification.

A competing power-law specification was proposed by
\citet{almgren2005direct}, who reject the square-root form in favour of
a $3/5$ exponent ($\Delta P \propto \sigma\,\eta\,(Q/V)^{0.6}$) using
a large proprietary institutional dataset. We do \emph{not} adopt the
Almgren et al.\ formulation; we implement the square-root law following
\citet{lillo2003master} for tractability and prevalence in prior research.
Appendix~\ref{app:power_law} reports a robustness check confirming that
our qualitative conclusions are unchanged under the $3/5$ power law.

\citet{almgren2001optimal} derive optimal liquidation trajectories under
temporary-plus-permanent impact, providing the theoretical basis for
participation-rate constraints. \citet{obizhaeva2013optimal} extend this
framework to incorporate limit-order-book resilience, providing a second
foundational optimal-execution model.

\subsection{Cost-Adjusted Strategy Performance}

\citet{novymarx2016taxonomy} systematically study after-cost performance
across a large set of anomalies using TAQ data, finding that strategies
with monthly one-way turnover above roughly 50\% rarely survive realistic
transaction costs. \citet{korajczyk2004momentum} test cross-sectional
momentum against intraday price-impact measures, estimating break-even
fund sizes of \$5 billion or more for liquidity-weighted implementations.
\citet{patton2020costs} extend Fama-MacBeth regressions to mutual fund
data, delivering all-in cost estimates that eliminate net-of-cost momentum
returns for the \emph{typical} mutual fund (though positive net returns
persist for the marginal fund investor). \citet{jegadeesh1993returns} document the
original cross-sectional momentum anomaly; \citet{moskowitz2012time}
extend it to time-series momentum across asset classes.
\citet{garleanu2013dynamic} provide the theoretical framework for
optimal dynamic portfolio construction under predictable returns and
transaction costs, directly relevant to the participation-rate
constraints studied here. The present paper
differs from these studies in three material respects: (i) it attributes
cost impact at each individual friction tier, providing a decomposition
unavailable in aggregate cost studies; (ii) it provides a formally-tested
open-source engine as a reusable artefact; and (iii) it focuses on ETF
strategies, where the liquidity regime differs materially from
individual-stock anomaly portfolios.

\subsection{Event-Driven Backtesting and Simulation Correctness}

Na\"{i}ve vectorised backtests that do not enforce timestamp ordering
can inadvertently introduce look-ahead bias when the same bar's open
and close are used without explicit temporal safeguards.
Event-driven architectures \citep{zipline} enforce sequencing of
market, signal, order, and fill events explicitly.
\citet{lopezdeprado2018advances} advocates event-driven design because
it forces precise specification of timing assumptions.
\citet{chan2013algorithmic} provides a practitioner treatment of
common backtest pitfalls.

fill-quantity bounds---which none of the alternatives provide
uniformly---and composable, independently-activatable cost tiers
enabling per-tier attribution.

\subsection{Sharpe Ratio Inference under Autocorrelation}

\citet{sharpe1994sharpe} defines the generalised Sharpe ratio used
throughout. \citet{lo2002statistics} demonstrates that the standard
annualised Sharpe (scaled by $\sqrt{252}$) materially overstates the
true Sharpe ratio when daily returns exhibit positive autocorrelation,
as is the case for momentum strategies. \citet{bailey2012sharpe} derive
the Probabilistic Sharpe Ratio, which conditions on skewness and excess
kurtosis. The moving-blocks bootstrap of \citet{kunsch1989jackknife}
provides a nonparametric approach to Sharpe CIs that preserves serial
dependence without distributional assumptions, and is distinct from
the stationary bootstrap of \citet{politis1994stationary} which
randomises block lengths. For block-length selection, we set $b = 20$
as the primary choice because TSMOM-60 returns exhibit meaningful
autocorrelation at lags up to 15--20 days \citep{lo2002statistics},
and report $b \in \{10, 30\}$ as robustness checks.

\subsection{Architecture}

\texttt{ExecutionHandler} (simulates fills under the configured friction
model). Within each trading day these activate in the fixed sequential

\end{sloppypar}

\subsection{Causality Enforcement}

The most common source of inadvertent look-ahead bias in daily backtests
is computing a signal using closing price $p^{\rm close}_t$ and then
filling at the same bar's open $p^{\rm open}_t$. In OHLCV data the open
is sequentially \emph{earlier} than the close; filling at the same-bar
open is therefore a form of look-ahead. The \texttt{DataHandler} prevents
this by enforcing that \texttt{get\_history\_asof}$(s, t)$ returns data
indexed only at $t' \le t$, implemented via a per-symbol monotonically
advancing timestamp pointer that prohibits backward movement.

\paragraph{Data-adjustment look-ahead.}
Code-level causality enforcement does not prevent retroactively adjusted
prices. If the data provider adjusts historical bars after corporate
actions, the as-of slice implicitly incorporates a future dividend or
split event. Section~\ref{sec:crossval} addresses this through
cross-source validation.

\subsection{Correctness Invariants and Test Suite}
\label{sec:invariants}

Four hard invariants are enforced by automated unit tests shipped with
the engine and run on every commit via CI: (i)~double-entry accounting
identity ($E_t = C_t + \sum_s q^s_t p^{s,\rm close}_t$, residual
$<10^{-8}$ normalised equity units); (ii)~strict data causality
(strategy sees only $t' \le t$); (iii)~fill-quantity bounds
($0 \le q^{\rm filled} \le q^{\rm ordered}$); and (iv)~cost
monotonicity on deterministic data. In addition, MDD values below
$-0.90$ trigger an automatic trade-level audit.
Full specification is in Appendix~\ref{app:invariants}.

\subsection{Portfolio Mechanics}

The portfolio uses a long-only, target-weight sizing rule. On a buy
signal for symbol $s$ at time $t$, the target position is:
\begin{equation}
  q^{\rm target}_s =
    \left\lfloor
      \frac{\min\!\left(w E_t,\; w_{\max} E_t,\;
             C_t + q^s_t \cdot p^{s,\rm close}_t\right)}
            {p^{s,\rm close}_t}
    \right\rfloor.
  \label{eq:target_qty}
\end{equation}
An order is generated only when $|\Delta q| \ge 1$ share. On a sell
signal, the full open position is liquidated. In all primary experiments
$w = w_{\max} = 1.0$ (fully invested, no leverage). The long-short
extension is discussed in Section~\ref{sec:longshort}.
When multiple buy signals arrive at the same timestamp, orders are
processed in deterministic FIFO symbol order as they are emitted by the
event queue; any residual cash shortfall is handled by share-level
down-sizing at fill time. This first-come-first-served rule is the
authoritative allocation convention used in all reported results.

\paragraph{M0: Na\"{i}ve.}
$p^{\rm fill} = p^{\rm open}_{t+1}$, total cost $= 0$. This is the
performance \emph{ceiling}, not a realistic baseline.

\paragraph{M1: Commission Fees.}
$\mathrm{fee} = (\kfee / 10{,}000) \cdot p^{\rm fill} \cdot q$.
We use 5 bps as an institutional proxy. Post-2019 retail ETF
commissions are nominally zero at major US brokers, but clearing,
regulatory, and market-data fees persist at 1--3 bps; 5 bps is
slightly conservative for retail and approximately correct for
mid-size institutional mandates.

\paragraph{M2: Bid--Ask Spread.}
BUY: $p^{\rm fill} = p^{\rm open}_{t+1} \cdot (1 + h/10{,}000)$.
SELL: $p^{\rm fill} = p^{\rm open}_{t+1} \cdot (1 - h/10{,}000)$.
The 10 bps label is the round-trip cost ($2h = 10$ bps). Quoted
half-spreads for SPY are typically 1--2 bps; 5 bps is conservative
for the full ten-ETF universe including less-liquid sector funds.

\paragraph{M3: Volatility-Scaled Slippage.}
$s_t = k_{\rm vol} \cdot \sigma_{{\rm ann},t}$ [bps], where
$\sigma_{\rm ann}$ is the 20-day rolling annualised close-to-close
volatility ($n-1$ d.f.) and $k_{\rm vol} = 10.0$. Fill:
$p^{\rm fill} = p^{\rm open}_{t+1} \cdot (1 \pm s_t/10{,}000)$,
positive for BUY, negative for SELL.

\paragraph{M4: Square-Root Market Impact.}
The square-root temporary impact model \citep{lillo2003master,bouchaud2009theory}:
\begin{equation}
  \iota_t = \kimp \cdot \sqrt{V_{\rm trade}/V_{\rm ADV}}
  \quad [\text{fraction}],
  \label{eq:impact}
\end{equation}
where $V_{\rm trade} = p^{\rm open}_{t+1} \cdot q$ and $V_{\rm ADV}$
is 20-day rolling average dollar volume. $\kimp = 0.50$ is our baseline; the sensitivity sweep
spans $\kimp \in \{0, 0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$
(Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity} and footnote~2).
The complete fill price is:
\begin{equation}
  p^{\rm fill} =
  \begin{cases}
    p^{\rm open}_{t+1}
      \cdot\!\left(1 + \frac{h}{10{,}000}\right)
      \cdot\!\left(1 + \frac{s_t + \iota_t^{\rm bps}}{10{,}000}\right)
    & \text{(BUY),}\\[8pt]
    p^{\rm open}_{t+1}
      \cdot\!\left(1 - \frac{h}{10{,}000}\right)
      \cdot\!\left(1 - \frac{s_t + \iota_t^{\rm bps}}{10{,}000}\right)
    & \text{(SELL),}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:fill}
\end{equation}
where $\iota_t^{\rm bps} = \iota_t \times 10{,}000$ and spread and
impact are applied multiplicatively (compounded).\footnote{%
The multiplicative form introduces a second-order cross-term
$h \cdot (s_t+\iota_t^{\rm bps})/10^8$. At the baseline calibration
($h=5$ bps, $s_t+\iota_t^{\rm bps}\lesssim 100$ bps), this term is
below $0.05$ bps per trade and is immaterial for the qualitative
conclusions.
Note on the exponent: \citet{almgren2005direct} \emph{reject}
the square-root model for temporary impact in favour of a $3/5$ power law,
$\Delta P \propto \sigma\,\eta\,(Q/V)^{0.6}$, normalised by
volatility $\sigma$. We do \emph{not} adopt the Almgren et al.\ formulation;
we implement the square-root law following \citet{lillo2003master} for
tractability and prevalence in prior research. The two specifications use
different normalisations ($\kimp$ vs.\ Almgren's $\eta$), so they are not
directly comparable in calibration.
Appendix~\ref{app:power_law} reports a robustness check confirming that
the qualitative conclusions are unchanged under the $3/5$ power law.
Researchers calibrating on proprietary TCA data should re-estimate
on their own trade population; see \citet{lillo2003master} for methodology.}

The participation-rate cap:
\begin{equation}
  q^{\rm filled} = \min\!\bigl(q^{\rm ordered},\;
    \lfloor \rho \cdot \mathrm{ADV}_{\rm shares} \rfloor\bigr),
  \label{eq:partfill}
\end{equation}
with $\rho = 5\%$ of 20-day average daily share volume.
Note that $V_{\rm ADV}$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:impact} is \emph{dollar} ADV
($= \overline{p^{\rm close} \cdot V_{\rm vol}}$, rolling 20-day mean
dollar volume), whereas $\mathrm{ADV}_{\rm shares}$ in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:partfill} is \emph{share} ADV
($= \overline{V_{\rm vol}}$, rolling 20-day mean share volume);
the two are related by current open price but represent distinct
quantities used for impact pricing versus quantity capping.
Section~\ref{sec:rho_sweep} reports a full sweep over
$\rho \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.20, 0.30\}$.
For the first 19 trading days (insufficient 20-day history),
slippage and impact terms are set to zero and the participation cap is
not binding; no burn-in rows are dropped from the backtest.

\paragraph{M5: Delayed Execution.}
As M4, but fills at $t{+}2$ open ($\delta = 2$), modelling one
additional day of operational latency in a daily-signal workflow.

cost differentials studied here, but this cannot be directly
quantified without a comprehensive database of liquidated ETFs.

\subsection{Strategies}
\label{sec:strategies}

Three strategies are studied, spanning a qualitative cost-sensitivity
spectrum: TSMOM-60 is borderline (retains a marginal positive Sharpe
through M4); CSMOM-60 is cost-fragile (sign-flips at M4); and
MeanRev-z1 is fatally cost-sensitive (sign-flips at M1).

\subsubsection{TSMOM-60: Time-Series Momentum}

Following \citet{moskowitz2012time}:
\begin{equation}
  r^{(60)}_{s,t} = p_{s,t}/p_{s,t-60} - 1.
  \label{eq:tsmom}
\end{equation}
Buy symbol $s$ when $r^{(60)}_{s,t} > 0$; liquidate otherwise.
Each ETF is evaluated independently; the strategy can hold 0--10
positions simultaneously. Estimated turnover: 15--20 round-trips
per year per symbol.

\subsubsection{CSMOM-60: Cross-Sectional Momentum}
\label{sec:csmom_spec}

Cross-sectional momentum \citep{jegadeesh1993returns} ranks assets
by their past $J$-period return and holds the top-$K$. We set $J = 60$
days and $K = 3$ (top 30\% of the ten-instrument universe), equally
weighted within the long basket:
\begin{equation}
  w^{\rm CSMOM}_{s,t} = \tfrac{1}{K} \cdot
    \mathbf{1}\!\left[\mathrm{rank}_{s,t}^{(60)} \ge N-K+1\right],
  \label{eq:csmom}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{rank}_{s,t}^{(60)}$ is the rank (1 = lowest) of symbol
$s$ among $N = 10$ symbols by their 60-day return at time $t$.
This construction follows the standard momentum lineage in
\citet{carhart1997persistence} and cross-asset evidence in
\citet{asness2013value}.

\textbf{Structural differences from TSMOM-60:}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \emph{Constant exposure}: CSMOM-60 always holds exactly three
    positions; TSMOM-60 can be fully out of the market.
  \item \emph{Relative signal}: CSMOM-60 exploits cross-sectional
    return dispersion rather than absolute-return direction.
  \item \emph{Turnover}: Rebalancing occurs only when top-3 membership
    changes, giving $\approx$25--35 round-trips per year---similar to
    TSMOM-60 but via a different mechanism.
  \item \emph{Literature bridge}: CSMOM-60 is directly comparable to
    \citet{jegadeesh1993returns} and \citet{novymarx2016taxonomy},
    providing a link between this ETF study and the individual-stock
    anomaly literature.
\end{enumerate}

Rank-boundary ties at the 7/8 threshold are broken by secondary sort
on prior-day return (descending). The signal uses $p_{s,t}$ (closing
price on bar $t$) for the 60-day return; fills execute at $t+1$ open
under M0--M4 and $t+2$ open under M5. There is no look-ahead in this
specification.

\subsubsection{MeanRev-z1: Cost-Sensitivity Diagnostic}

\begin{equation}
  z_t = (r_t - \hat\mu_t)/\hat\sigma_t,
  \label{eq:meanrev}
\end{equation}
where $\hat\mu_t$ and $\hat\sigma_t$ are the sample mean and standard
deviation ($n-1$ d.f.) of the preceding 20 daily returns. Buy when
$z_t < -1.0$; liquidate otherwise.

MeanRev-z1 is included \emph{explicitly as a cost-sensitivity
diagnostic}, not as a viable strategy proposal. Its na\"{i}ve Sharpe
of 0.149 spans zero, and its 180--200 annual round-trips make it
predisposed to sign-flip under any meaningful cost. Its primary value
is as a calibration instrument: a strategy that sign-flips at M1
establishes the minimum cost sensitivity of the execution ladder and
provides a diagnostic bound on the per-trade cost threshold below which
the na\"{i}ve Sharpe is qualitatively misleading. ETF-level
daily return reversal faces structural headwinds: \citet{ben2012exchange}
document that ETF arbitrage activity injects nonfundamental volatility
into underlying securities, consistent with rapid mean-reversion of
any residual mispricing (author's inference from that review). The signal is
theoretically weak even before costs.

\subsection{Performance Metrics and Inference}
\label{sec:inference}

\begin{align}
  \hat{S} &= (\bar{r}/\hat\sigma) \cdot \sqrt{252}
    \quad\text{(annualised Sharpe \citep{sharpe1994sharpe}),}
    \label{eq:sharpe}\\
  \mathrm{CAGR} &= (E_T/E_0)^{1/Y} - 1,
    \label{eq:cagr}\\
  \mathrm{MDD} &= \min_t\bigl(E_t/\textstyle\max_{s \le t} E_s - 1\bigr).
    \label{eq:mdd}
\end{align}

\textbf{Block-bootstrap CIs.} We use the moving-blocks bootstrap of
\citet{kunsch1989jackknife}:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Primary:} $b = 20$ days, $N = 1{,}000$ resamples,
    seed 42. This is our headline specification; $b = 20$ spans
    the autocorrelation horizon of momentum returns.
  \item \textbf{Robustness:} $b = 10$ and $b = 30$, same $N$ and seed.
    Both are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:bootstrap_sensitivity}.
\end{itemize}

We do not use the stationary bootstrap of \citet{politis1994stationary},
which draws random block lengths. We prefer the moving-blocks bootstrap
for its determinism given a fixed seed. The key qualitative conclusions
are robust across all three block lengths; under $b = 20$ the CIs are
approximately 8--10\% wider than under $b = 10$, only strengthening
the conclusion that, at $J=60$, no active strategy achieves a
post-cost CI entirely above zero (TSMOM and CSMOM have pre-cost M0/M1
CIs entirely positive, but these collapse to span zero by M4;
TSMOM-120 is a partial exception---see
Section~\ref{sec:lookback_main}).

\textbf{Statistical interpretation terminology.}
We distinguish three CI outcomes:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep, label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textit{CI entirely above zero} ($\ell > 0$, no $^\dagger$):
    point estimate is statistically positive; evidence of genuine
    positive performance. We further note that ``robustly positive''
    requires $\ell \ge +0.05$ with comfortable margin from zero;
    TSMOM M2 ($\ell = +0.003$) is technically in this category but
    should be treated as a boundary case.
  \item \textit{CI spanning zero} ($\ell \le 0 \le u$, $^\dagger$):
    point estimate is statistically indistinguishable from zero.
  \item \textit{CI entirely below zero} ($u < 0$, $^\ddagger$):
    negative performance is statistically robust; this is a
    \emph{stronger} negative statement than merely spanning zero.
    MeanRev-z1's CIs are in this category from M1 onward.
\end{enumerate}
This is consistent with the well-known non-Gaussian, autocorrelated
properties of financial returns \citep{cont2001empirical}.

\textbf{Sharpe inflation ratio:}
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{IR}_{Mk} = \hat S_{M0} / \hat S_{Mk},
  \label{eq:ir}
\end{equation}
defined only when both Sharpes share positive sign.

\subsection{Sub-Period Windows}

Four evaluation windows: full sample (2005--2025), pre-/during-GFC
(2005--2012), mid-cycle expansion (2013--2019), and COVID-era and
recovery (2020--2025). The 2020--2025 window explicitly includes
the March 2020 drawdown.

\subsection{Passive Benchmark}

An equally-weighted buy-and-hold portfolio over all ten instruments,
rebalanced annually, evaluated under M0 and M4. M0 Sharpe $\approx 0.48$,
$b = 20$ CI $[+0.09, +0.87]$, CAGR $\approx 10\%$.
This benchmarking choice follows the convention of comparing
strategy performance against a simple passive alternative after
accounting for execution costs \citep{korajczyk2004momentum,novymarx2016taxonomy}.

\subsection{TSMOM-60: Monotonic Degradation}
\label{sec:tsmom_results}

TSMOM-60 degrades monotonically as execution assumptions become more
realistic. Under M0 it reports Sharpe 0.526, CI $[+0.11, +0.97]$, and
CAGR 7.8\%. \textit{Comparison to published results}: \citet{moskowitz2012time}
report Sharpe ratios of $0.36$--$0.56$ for time-series momentum across
diverse asset classes over 1985--2009, with equity-only strategies
at the lower end of this range. Our M0 Sharpe of 0.526 is within their
reported range, providing a basic sanity check; note that our universe
(ten US equity ETFs) differs from Moskowitz et al.'s multi-asset,
long-short implementation, so direct comparability is limited.
Three additional baseline observations warrant emphasis before
discussing cost attribution.

First, the CAGR of 7.8\% falls below the passive buy-and-hold
benchmark's 10.0\% over the same period: the active strategy
underperforms passive \emph{before} realistic costs are applied.

Second, \finding{the M0 Sharpe CI is positive in this canonical run, but
widens and approaches zero in higher-friction tiers.} The wide CI reflects the high variance of daily
momentum returns over a 20-year sample and the conservative block
length, which preserves the full autocorrelation structure.

Third, CIs under M0 and M1 are entirely positive; M2's lower bound
($+0.003$, rounded to $+0.00$ in tables) is essentially zero; M3--M5
span zero. The widening reflects genuine statistical imprecision from
estimating strategy performance over a single 20-year daily return series.

Adding fees (M1) reduces Sharpe to 0.475; spread (M2) yields 0.431;
slippage (M3) yields 0.413. The M1--M3 degradation is gradual and
approximately linear, reflecting constant per-trade cost burden across
15--20 annual round-trips.

Square-root market impact (M4) produces a sharp
discontinuity: Sharpe falls from 0.413 (M3) to 0.020 (M4), CAGR
turns negative ($-1.1\%$), and MDD deteriorates to $-70.4\%$.
The Sharpe inflation ratio is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{IR}_{M4} = \hat{S}_{M0}/\hat{S}_{M4} = 0.526/0.020
  \approx 25.7\times.
\end{equation}
\noindent\textit{Note on rounding} (at $\kimp=0.50$, $\rho=0.05$): the $25.7\times$ ratio uses
full-precision engine values ($0.52603\ldots$ and $0.02048\ldots$);
dividing the rounded display values ($0.526/0.020$) yields $26.3\times$.
All inflation ratios in Table~\ref{tab:inflation} use full-precision Sharpes.
The M4 CI $[-0.46, +0.48]$ spans zero.
\finding{Under impact-constrained execution, TSMOM-60 performance is
statistically indistinguishable from zero; the na\"{i}ve backtest's
positive point estimate is statistical noise within a wide
uncertainty band (CI width: 0.94 Sharpe units).}

The M4 discontinuity has a single root cause: the convex square-root
impact law applied to TSMOM-60's characteristically large entry
orders. (i) The square-root law is \emph{convex}: doubling the traded
fraction of ADV more than doubles the cost. (ii) TSMOM-60's binary
entry rule (flat to 100\% weight in a single step) generates large
single-step orders that disproportionately activate the convex impact
relative to M1--M3 incremental rebalancing. At this portfolio scale
(\$100K), the 5\% participation cap is non-binding (fill fraction
$\approx 100\%$ across all orders; see Table~\ref{tab:rho_sweep}),
so there are no multi-day fill ladders: the entire M4 discontinuity
is attributable to the convex impact function alone. This creates a
cost regime qualitatively different from M1--M3 and not approximable
by linearly scaling the lower-tier costs.

The M3-to-M4 Sharpe drop ($0.413 \to 0.020$, a fall of 0.393) is
approximately $22\times$ larger than the M2-to-M3 drop ($0.431 \to
0.413$, a fall of 0.018), even though both represent adding a single
friction tier. This non-linearity is the central empirical finding for
TSMOM-60 and motivates the per-tier attribution framework as a research
requirement rather than an optional enhancement.

Adding the two-day delay (M5) moves Sharpe from 0.020 to $-0.014$.
The marginal effect of the additional delay is small: the 60-day signal
decays slowly, so one additional execution day has minimal signal-decay
cost relative to the already substantial M4 impact and participation
drag.

\subsection{CSMOM-60: A Statistically Positive Pre-Cost Signal That
Still Degrades Materially}
\label{sec:csmom_results}

CSMOM-60 provides the qualitatively distinct third case in this study.
Under M0 it reports Sharpe 0.583, $b = 20$ CI $[+0.20, +1.00]$, and
CAGR 9.1\%. \textit{Comparison to published results}: \citet{jegadeesh1993returns}
document 12-month cross-sectional momentum returns of
$\approx 1\%$ per month (roughly Sharpe $0.5$--$0.7$ depending on the period
and implementation) in US equity portfolios over 1965--1989; their universe
and formation rules differ materially from our ten-ETF implementation.
Our CSMOM-60 M0 Sharpe of 0.583 is broadly consistent with this range,
though again direct comparability is limited by universe and sample differences.

\finding{Both CSMOM-60 and TSMOM-60 show clearly positive pre-cost CI
lower bounds at M0 and M1 in the canonical run; CSMOM yields the wider
lower bound at M0 ($+0.20$ vs.\ $+0.11$ for TSMOM).} For CSMOM, the M1
estimate is Sharpe 0.468 with CI $[+0.09, +0.88]$, so a modest fee tier
does not remove the pre-cost signal.

At M2 (+spread), Sharpe falls to 0.356 with CI $[-0.03, +0.76]$, so the
lower bound crosses zero. M3 is similar (0.315, CI $[-0.07, +0.72]$).
The key transition occurs at M4: Sharpe becomes negative at $-0.242$
with CI $[-0.62, +0.18]$, and CAGR also turns negative ($-5.7\%$).

The implication is stronger than in the first-round draft: CSMOM's
execution degradation is not merely a reduction in magnitude but a
directional sign flip at the impact-constrained tier. \finding{A robust
pre-cost signal does not guarantee positive post-cost performance once
participation limits and impact are enforced.} This is the core value of
the tiered attribution framework.

\subsection{MeanRev-z1: Diagnostic of Fatal Cost Sensitivity}
\label{sec:meanrev_results}

Under M0, MeanRev-z1 Sharpe is 0.149 with CI $[-0.24, +0.54]$.
\finding{The na\"{i}ve Sharpe spans zero: MeanRev-z1 has no
statistically distinguishable gross edge even before any costs
are applied.} ETF-level daily return reversal faces structural headwinds
consistent with ETF arbitrage activity documented by \citet{ben2012exchange};
the signal is theoretically weak.

Adding fees alone (M1) drives Sharpe from $+0.149$ to $-0.500$, a sign
reversal. The $b = 20$ CI $[-0.88, -0.14]$ is entirely negative.
The arithmetic is direct: 180 round-trips per year at 10 bps
round-trip cost equals 1{,}800 bps ($= 18\%$) per year in commission
drag alone, against a gross annual edge of approximately 1.1\%.

\finding{A sign flip at M1 is the execution ladder's most severe
diagnostic outcome: the na\"{i}ve backtest is providing qualitatively
wrong information about the direction of expected performance. This
is detectable from a five-basis-point fee, the first and cheapest cost
tier.} Researchers can use this pattern as a sufficient condition for
immediate rejection before any further development effort.

The M4-to-M5 non-monotonicity ($-2.246$ to $-1.732$) is counterintuitive.
Trade-log diagnostics on the full sample show 52{,}620 signals in both
models, with M4 executing 7{,}219 fills and M5 executing 7{,}660 fills
(M5 executes \emph{more} fills, ruling out a trade-count reduction
as the mechanism). The pattern is consistent across all three
non-overlapping sub-periods: pre-GFC/GFC M4 $-3.42$ vs.\ M5 $-2.49$;
mid-cycle M4 $-4.87$ vs.\ M5 $-4.04$; COVID-era M4 $-2.94$ vs.\ M5
$-2.13$. Consistency across sub-periods rules out a sampling artifact.
The most plausible mechanism is a fill-price effect: for a
mean-reversion strategy, the t+2 open price has partially reverted
toward equilibrium relative to the t+1 open. This shifts the
entry and exit fills by one day along the mean-reversion arc, reducing
the \emph{realised} round-trip cost relative to the counterfactual t+1
execution at the extreme price. The accounting identity verifies the
result is numerically genuine; a full trade-level cost decomposition
quantifying this effect is beyond the present study's scope. This
behavior does not contradict the controlled monotonicity invariant in
Section~\ref{sec:invariants}, which is defined for deterministic
single-symbol test data rather than this stochastic, multi-symbol setting.

regime-specific.

\subsection{The Passive Benchmark as the Appropriate Null}

The na\"{i}ve TSMOM CAGR (7.8\%) falls below the passive benchmark
($\approx 10\%$) before any realistic cost. Under M4, TSMOM CAGR turns
negative ($-1.1\%$). The passive buy-and-hold benchmark has a $b = 20$ CI of $[+0.09,\;+0.87]$,
entirely above zero. Pre-cost (M0, M1) rows for TSMOM and CSMOM are also
entirely above zero, but at $J=60$ no active strategy maintains a CI
entirely above zero once realistic costs (M2 onward) are applied
(TSMOM-120 partially survives M4; Section~\ref{sec:lookback_main}).
Passive investment over this universe and period is statistically
distinguishable from zero; at the canonical $J=60$ lookback, no active
strategy is, under any cost-realistic execution model.

This makes the passive benchmark, rather than a zero-Sharpe null,
the appropriate comparison point for active strategy claims. A strategy
should not merely exceed zero expected Sharpe; it should exceed passive
Sharpe with statistical confidence. No strategy in this study clears
this bar under realistic execution.

CSMOM-60's M0 and M1 CIs have positive lower bounds, but
their upper bounds also do not significantly exceed the passive
CI's range ($[+0.09, +0.87]$). A researcher claiming CSMOM-60
generates alpha over passive would need to test this claim explicitly
rather than comparing Sharpe point estimates.

\subsection{Why Market Impact Creates a Discontinuity}
\label{sec:discontinuity}

The M3-to-M4 performance drop is qualitatively discontinuous
for both TSMOM and CSMOM. The square-root impact law is
\emph{convex}: the marginal cost of additional trading increases
with trade size, creating a superlinear relationship between
order size and cost burden. Strategy-specific trade structure
amplifies this convex cost: TSMOM-60's binary signal
(flat-to-fully-invested entry) generates larger single-step trade
sizes than CSMOM-60's incremental cross-sectional rebalancing.
At the \$100K portfolio scale in this study, the 5\% participation
cap is non-binding (fill fraction $\approx 100\%$ for all orders;
Table~\ref{tab:rho_sweep})---the cap begins to matter only at
portfolio sizes above $\sim$\$50M---so the
entire discontinuity is attributable to the convex impact cost alone.
\label{sec:discussion_cap}

The M4 degradation asymmetry remains clear: TSMOM retains a small
positive Sharpe at M4 ($25.7\times$ inflation from M0, at $\kimp=0.50$), while CSMOM
sign-flips negative at M4.
When TSMOM re-enters after a flat period, it attempts to go from
zero to 100\% weight in a single step, generating a trade that is
large relative to typical incremental rebalancing and therefore
disproportionately costly under the convex square-root model.
CSMOM's constant rebalancing from approximately-equal weights produces
smaller marginal trades per rebalancing event, yet its higher
turnover frequency still accumulates sufficient impact to flip the
sign at M4. This structural insight is identifiable only through
per-tier attribution; it is invisible in either the M0 or
aggregate M4 comparison.

\subsection{The Three-Strategy Hierarchy as a Research Diagnostic}
\label{sec:hierarchy}

The three strategies provide a diagnostic hierarchy that the execution
ladder makes explicit and quantifiable:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{MeanRev-z1} (sign flip at M1): The na\"{i}ve Sharpe
    is providing directionally wrong information. Structural cause:
    turnover arithmetic. Detection: immediate from M1 Sharpe sign.
    Appropriate action: immediate rejection.

  \item \textbf{TSMOM-60} (entirely positive CI at M0--M1, spanning
    zero from M2, collapse at M4): The na\"{i}ve Sharpe is providing
    quantitatively misleading information. The pre-cost signal is too
    weak to absorb M4 costs. Structural cause: binary-entry rule
    generating large single-step trades under the convex square-root
    impact function. Detection: requires the
    M4 tier. Appropriate action: sizing reduction or signal
    modification before further development.

  \item \textbf{CSMOM-60} (positive pre-cost, sign flip at M4): The na\"{i}ve
    Sharpe is providing qualitatively correct directional information
    but overstates post-cost viability once impact is active. Appropriate
    action: explicit participation-cap and impact sensitivity analysis
    before deployment.
\end{enumerate}

This hierarchy illustrates that the execution ladder is not merely
an academic exercise: it determines whether a researcher should
reject, modify, or conditionally accept a strategy based on
specific, attributable mechanisms rather than vague references to
``execution risk.''

\subsection{Statistical Precision and the CI Imperative}
\label{sec:stats_precision}

The $b = 20$ bootstrap CIs carry a message beyond any individual
strategy result. Under $b = 20$, TSMOM and CSMOM are statistically
positive at pre-cost tiers (M0, M1) but collapse to span zero or turn
robustly negative under realistic costs. Specifically: TSMOM M2--M5
and CSMOM M2--M5 span zero; MeanRev M0 spans zero; MeanRev M1--M5
are robustly negative. At $J=60$, no active strategy maintains a CI
entirely above zero past M1. A Sharpe point estimate reported without
a CI is
incomplete information. The 20-year daily sample, with the
autocorrelation structure of momentum returns and conservative
$b = 20$ blocks, does not provide sufficient statistical power
to distinguish any active strategy from zero after realistic execution.

The $b = 10$ results in Appendix~\ref{app:bootstrap_sensitivity}
are qualitatively similar but have CIs approximately 8\% narrower.
The danger of $b = 10$ is that it can overstate precision in borderline
cases. In the canonical run, CSMOM M0 and M1 remain positive at
$b = 30$, while CSMOM M2 and M4 remain non-positive. The key inference
is therefore stable across tested block lengths: pre-cost signal can be
statistically positive while post-cost viability still fails.

\subsection{Long-Short Extension}
\label{sec:longshort}

This paper reports only long-only primary results. Although the engine
architecture can be extended to long-short portfolios, we do not report
long-short estimates here and therefore make no quantitative claims
about short-leg performance or borrow-cost-adjusted alpha. The key
implication is scope: all conclusions in Sections~\ref{sec:results}
and~\ref{sec:discussion} are specific to long-only implementations.

Future work should report a fully specified long-short CSMOM design
(position limits, borrow-cost model, locate failures, and short
participation constraints) using the same M0--M5 attribution framework.

\subsection{Non-Stationarity of Microstructure}

Market microstructure has changed substantially over 2005--2025.
Spread compression from increased competition among market makers
and the proliferation of high-frequency trading has reduced quoted
half-spreads for large-cap ETFs from approximately 5--10 bps in
2005 to 1--2 bps by 2020. Our constant $h = 5$ bps assumption is
therefore more conservative in recent periods than in the
pre-HFT era, and a time-varying spread specification would reduce
M2 costs in post-2015 data relative to our estimates.

Conversely, the market-impact coefficient $\kimp$ may have changed
over time as participation, liquidity supply, and crowding conditions
evolve. Our constant-$\kimp$ specification should therefore be read as
a tractable baseline rather than a structural claim about temporal
stability.
A rolling-window analysis of how the M3-to-M4 discontinuity varies
over time would strengthen the results; we leave this to future work.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
%% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\paragraph{Daily-bar granularity.}
All friction models are stylised proxies for intraday dynamics.
Queue priority, limit-order book depth, the intraday profile of
market impact (which peaks at open and close), and the correlation
between signal arrival and impact costs are not captured.
Tick-level simulation is required for precise institutional cost
estimation.

\paragraph{Impact model calibration.}
The $25.7\times$ TSMOM inflation ratio is conditional on $\kimp = 0.50$
and $\rho = 0.05$. The sensitivity sweep (Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity})
spans $\kimp \in \{0, 0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$ and the
participation sweep spans $\rho \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.20, 0.30\}$;
both should be consulted before interpreting any single inflation
ratio as representative. Researchers with proprietary TCA data should
re-calibrate both parameters using institutional TCA methodology
\citep[see][]{lillo2003master} on their own trade population.

\paragraph{Universe, period, and strategy parameterisation.}
Results derive from ten US equity ETFs over 2005--2025. A critical
specification finding (Section~\ref{sec:lookback_main}): TSMOM-120
achieves M4 Sharpe $+0.276$ at $\kimp=0.50$, demonstrating
that the claim ``no active strategy survives M4'' holds for $J=60$
but not universally. The lookback window is a primary design lever
for impact-constrained momentum strategies, operating through
its effect on turnover and hence aggregate impact drag.
Full lookback and CSMOM top-$K$ sensitivity results are in
Appendix~\ref{app:lookback_k_sensitivity}. Remaining future-work
ablations include z-score threshold sensitivity for MeanRev
($z \in \{0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$) and alternative universes
(sector ETFs only, international, fixed income).

\paragraph{Long-only constraint and MeanRev cost overstatement.}
The full-liquidation-on-signal rule for MeanRev-z1 creates artificially
high turnover relative to a persistent-position implementation,
likely overstating its cost sensitivity. This is a deliberate design
choice for the diagnostic use case, but it limits the generalisability
of MeanRev-z1 cost estimates to implementations that do not liquidate
on every signal.

\paragraph{Data adjustment look-ahead.}
Total-return adjusted prices incorporate the full corporate-action
history as of the download date, which is not available in real time.
Cross-validation against Stooq (Section~\ref{sec:crossval}) confirms
that vendor-specific artifacts are minimal, but the adjustment
look-ahead is irreducible without intraday data.

\paragraph{Rebalancing frequency and sizing design levers (Appendix~\ref{app:ablation_freq}).}
Two natural design modifications to TSMOM-60 substantially alter the
impact-resilience profile.
(i)~\textit{Monthly rebalancing}: trading only on the last trading day
of each month reduces trade frequency and order size relative to ADV,
cutting impact drag. The M4 Sharpe rises from $+0.020$ (daily,
canonical) to $+0.230$ (monthly) at the same $\kimp = 0.50$---an
$11.5\times$ improvement---while M0 Sharpe falls modestly from $+0.526$
to $+0.423$ (lower signal refresh rate).
(ii)~\textit{Volatility-targeted sizing}: scaling position weight by
$\min(\sigma^*/\hat\sigma_t, 1.5)$ (target $\sigma^* = 10\%$, 20-day
lookback) slightly reduces M0 Sharpe to $+0.473$ and M4 Sharpe to $-0.081$,
because the leverage multiplier amplifies the convex impact cost when the
signal fires during low-vol environments. See Appendix~\ref{app:ablation_freq}
for full results. Trading frequency is therefore a more tractable design
lever for impact-constrained momentum strategies than vol-scaling at
$\$100$K scale.

\paragraph{Autocorrelation-unadjusted Sharpe point estimates.}
\citet{lo2002statistics} shows that the na\"{i}ve annualised Sharpe
overstates the true Sharpe ratio when returns exhibit positive
autocorrelation. The $b = 20$ bootstrap CIs address this for inference;
point estimates themselves are unadjusted. An autocorrelation-corrected
Sharpe for TSMOM-60 would reduce the M0 point estimate, only
strengthening the conclusion that, at $J=60$, no active strategy
achieves a post-cost CI entirely above zero (MeanRev-z1 post-M1 CIs
are robustly entirely negative, a stronger statement than spanning
zero).

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The opening scenario---a backtested Sharpe of 0.5 that evaporates in
live trading---is not a rare failure mode. It follows predictably from
the combination of near-zero gross edge per trade, positive turnover,
and execution assumptions that ignore the primary cost channels of
institutional trading. This paper demonstrates, on a controlled
ten-ETF case study, that the Sharpe inflation ratio between na\"{i}ve
and impact-constrained execution reaches $25.7\times$ (at $\kimp=0.50$,
$\rho=0.05$; sensitivity sweep: $\sim$$1.3\times$--$25.7\times$ across
$\kimp \in [0, 0.50]$) for a strategy with genuine time-series momentum
signal (TSMOM-60), while a
cross-sectional momentum strategy with positive pre-cost CI (CSMOM-60)
sign-flips under M4, and MeanRev-z1 produces a directional sign flip
at the first cost tier.
TSMOM-60 and CSMOM-60 post-impact CIs span zero; MeanRev-z1
post-fee CIs are robustly entirely negative throughout. At the
canonical $J=60$ lookback, no active strategy maintains a CI entirely
above zero past the first realistic cost tier (M1); TSMOM-120
partially survives M4 (Section~\ref{sec:lookback_main}).

Three strategic lessons follow from these results.

\textit{First}, pre-cost statistical significance is necessary but not
sufficient. CSMOM-60 is statistically positive pre-cost
($b = 20$ CI lower bound $+0.20$), yet its M4 Sharpe turns negative
($-0.242$) with CI spanning zero. A researcher who stops at the
pre-cost evaluation would misclassify CSMOM-60 as viable; the execution
ladder reveals that convex market impact alone can
fully consume the pre-cost edge (at \$100K scale the participation cap
is non-binding; Table~\ref{tab:rho_sweep}).

\textit{Second}, the M3-to-M4 discontinuity is the critical threshold.
The difference between TSMOM-60 (large positive inflation ratio at M4)
and CSMOM-60 (M4 sign flip) reflects a structural difference in order-sizing mechanics, not
in the underlying signal strength. Researchers can use per-tier attribution
to identify whether a strategy's M4 degradation stems from high turnover
(fee and spread dominant, diagnosable at M1--M2), high per-trade impact
(square-root dominant, diagnosable at M4 with $\kimp$ sweep), or
fill-laddering (participation-cap dominant, diagnosable via the
$\rho$ sweep). These diagnoses lead to different remediation strategies:
turnover reduction, signal smoothing, or position-size management
respectively.

\textit{Third}, the passive benchmark should always be reported.
At $J=60$, no active strategy in this study---at any execution tier,
under any bootstrap block length---achieves a CI entirely above zero.
The passive portfolio does. Researchers claiming active value-add must compare
against the passive benchmark with appropriate statistical inference,
not against a zero-Sharpe null.

These findings depend on the specific parameters and universe used and
should not be generalised without further empirical validation. They do,
however, establish a reusable methodological framework: a formally
verified, composable, open-source backtesting engine with per-tier
attribution, block-bootstrap CIs, a cross-source validation protocol,
and an MDD audit diagnostic. These tools make credible cost-aware
backtesting accessible without requiring production-scale infrastructure.

Install dependencies (\texttt{pip install -r requirements.txt}),

\section{Engine Loop Pseudocode}
\label{app:pseudocode}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Event-Driven Backtest Loop (one trading day)}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} $\mathcal{D}$ (price data), $\mathcal{S}$
  (strategy), $\mathcal{P}$ (portfolio), $\mathcal{E}$ (execution)
\For{each trading day $t$}
  \State $\mathcal{P}.\texttt{update\_prices}(t, \mathcal{D})$
  \State $\textit{orders} \leftarrow [\ ]$
  \For{each symbol $s$}
    \State $e_M \leftarrow \texttt{MarketEvent}(t, s)$
    \State $e_\sigma \leftarrow
           \mathcal{S}.\texttt{on\_market}(e_M,\;
           \mathcal{D}.\texttt{asof}(s,t))$
           \Comment{Data restricted to $t' \le t$}
    \If{$e_\sigma \ne \emptyset$}
      \State $e_O \leftarrow \mathcal{P}.\texttt{on\_signal}(e_\sigma)$
      \If{$e_O \ne \emptyset$}
        \State $\textit{orders}.\texttt{append}(e_O)$
      \EndIf
    \EndIf
  \EndFor
  \State \Comment{All orders batched before fills: symbol-A fill
    cannot affect cash available for symbol-B order in the same bar.}
  \For{each $e_O$ in $\textit{orders}$}
    \State $e_F \leftarrow \mathcal{E}.\texttt{execute}(e_O,\mathcal{D})$
           \Comment{Fill at bar $t+\delta$ open}
    \If{$e_F \ne \emptyset$}
      \State $\mathcal{P}.\texttt{on\_fill}(e_F)$
    \EndIf
  \EndFor
  \State $\mathcal{P}.\texttt{mark\_to\_market}(t, \mathcal{D})$
  \State \Comment{Verify Eq.~\eqref{eq:accounting}: residual $< 10^{-8}$
    in normalised equity units.}
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Correctness Invariants: Full Specification}
\label{app:invariants}

Four invariants are verified by automated unit tests shipped with the
engine and run on every commit via CI.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), nosep]
  \item \textbf{Accounting identity.} At every timestamp $t$:
    \begin{equation}
      E_t = C_t + \textstyle\sum_{s} q^s_t \cdot p^{s,\rm close}_t,
      \label{eq:accounting}
    \end{equation}
    where $E_t$ is total equity, $C_t$ is cash, $q^s_t$ is shares held
    in symbol $s$, and $p^{s,\rm close}_t$ is the closing price used for
    end-of-day mark-to-market. Fills execute at the next bar's open;
    mark-to-market reflects the signal-day close before the fill.
    The identity residual is verified $< 10^{-8}$ in normalised equity
    units at every time step.

  \item \textbf{Strict causality.} Strategy computation during bar $t$
    may access data only at indices $t' \le t$.

  \item \textbf{Fill-quantity bound.}
    $0 \le q^{\rm filled} \le q^{\rm ordered}$ for every fill.
    Impact-adjusted fill \emph{price} can exceed the observed high;
    the invariant constrains quantity, not price.

  \item \textbf{Cost monotonicity.} In a controlled single-symbol
    long-only test on deterministic data, activating any cost parameter
    cannot strictly increase total return.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{MDD audit diagnostic.}
Separately from the hard invariants, MDD values below $-0.90$ trigger
an automatic trade-level audit log that decomposes the equity-curve path
into gross-return and per-tier cost contributions. This eliminates a
class of artifacts where rounding errors in partial fills corrupt the
equity curve without affecting Sharpe. Section~\ref{sec:mdd_decomp}
applies this diagnostic to MeanRev-z1 under M4.

\section{Block-Length Sensitivity of Bootstrap CIs}
\label{app:bootstrap_sensitivity}

\subsection{Procedure}

Let $\{r_1, \ldots, r_T\}$ be the daily return series with
$T \approx 5{,}040$ (2005--2025). The 95\% CI for $\hat S$ is
constructed by: (i) drawing $\lceil T/b \rceil$ start indices i.i.d.
$\mathrm{Uniform}\{1, \ldots, T{-}b{+}1\}$ with replacement;
(ii) forming pseudo-sample $r^* = [r_{s_1}, \ldots, r_{s_1+b-1},
\ldots]$ truncated to length $T$;
(iii) computing $\hat S^* = (\bar r^*/\hat\sigma^*)\sqrt{252}$;
(iv) repeating $N = 1{,}000$ times with seed 42; (v) reporting the
2.5th and 97.5th percentiles. The primary $b = 20$ is chosen because
TSMOM return autocorrelation is meaningful at lags up to 15--20 days.

\section{CSMOM-60 Implementation Specification}
\label{app:csmom_spec}

\paragraph{Signal computation.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep, label=\arabic*.]
  \item At bar $t$, compute 60-day return for each symbol $s$:
    $r^{(60)}_{s,t} = p_{s,t}/p_{s,t-60} - 1$.
  \item Rank all $N=10$ symbols by $r^{(60)}_{s,t}$ ascending
    (rank 1 = lowest, rank 10 = highest).
  \item Signal is long for symbols with rank $\ge 8$ (top-3 of 10).
    Ties at the 7/8 boundary broken by secondary sort on prior-day
    return (descending).
  \item Target weight $w_s = 1/3$ for each long symbol; $w_s = 0$
    otherwise.
  \item Signals are recomputed each bar; portfolio target quantities are
    re-evaluated each bar under the same FIFO order-processing rule.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Look-ahead protection.}
The signal uses $p_{s,t}$ (closing price, bar $t$) and
$p_{s,t-60}$ (closing price, bar $t-60$). Both are available
from \texttt{get\_history\_asof}$(s, t)$ without look-ahead.
Fills execute at $t+1$ open (M0--M4) or $t+2$ open (M5).

\paragraph{Reproducibility command.}
\begin{verbatim}
python run_backtest.py \
  --strategy CSMOM --lookback 60 --top_k 3 \
  --model_range M0 M5 \
  --bootstrap_b 20 --bootstrap_n 1000 --seed 42 \
  --output results/csmom60_b20.json
\end{verbatim}

\section{MDD Audit Log Procedure}
\label{app:mdd_audit}

The engine's MDD audit is triggered automatically when MDD $< -0.90$.
The audit log records, for every trading day $t$:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item $E_t$, $C_t$, $\sum_s q^s_t \cdot p^{s,\rm close}_t$
    (Eq.~\ref{eq:accounting} components) and the accounting-identity
    residual (verified $< 10^{-8}$).
  \item Running maximum $\max_{s \le t} E_s$ and current drawdown.
  \item Gross P\&L contribution and per-tier cost drag for the day.
\end{itemize}

Run via:
\begin{verbatim}
make mdd_audit STRATEGY=MeanRev MODEL=M4
\end{verbatim}
The residual diagnostic for MeanRev-z1 M4 confirms that MDD $= -0.999$
is attributable to genuine cost arithmetic (Table~\ref{tab:mdd_decomp})
rather than a simulation artifact. The identity residual is
$< 10^{-8}$ at every time step in the trace.

\section{Zero-Commission Sensitivity}
\label{app:zero_commission}

To isolate the contribution of the 5\,bps round-trip commission (M1
tier) from the remaining execution costs, we re-run the M1--M5 ladder
for all three strategies with \texttt{fee\_bps\,=\,0} while holding
all other parameters at their canonical values.

\paragraph{TSMOM-60.}
Removing fees shifts M4 Sharpe from $0.020$ to $+0.061$
($\Delta = +0.041$). TSMOM retains a positive M4 Sharpe even in the
canonical run; fees account for only $\approx 7\%$ of the total
M0-to-M4 degradation ($0.041 / (0.526 - 0.020) \approx 8\%$).
The dominant cost is spread and impact.

\paragraph{CSMOM-60.}
Removing fees shifts M4 Sharpe from $-0.242$ to $-0.157$
($\Delta = +0.085$). CSMOM still sign-flips at M4 even without
fees, confirming that the directional sign change is driven by
spread and impact rather than the commission tier.

\paragraph{MeanRev-z1.}
Removing fees shifts M4 Sharpe from $-2.246$ to $-1.917$
($\Delta = +0.331$). The fee contribution is larger here because
MeanRev's high turnover ($\approx 180$ round-trips/year)
amplifies the per-trade fee drag. Nevertheless, the strategy is
still catastrophically cost-sensitive at M4 without fees.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
For TSMOM and CSMOM, the 5\,bps commission is a minor cost
component; the primary execution risk is spread and square-root
impact. For high-turnover strategies like MeanRev, per-trade
fees scale with turnover and become a material drag even at
institutional rates. Full results are in
\texttt{outputs/tables/zero\_commission\_sensitivity.csv}.

\item CAGR and MDD are point estimates; no bootstrap CIs are reported

\end{tablenotes}
\end{table}

\finding{Trading frequency is the dominant design lever for
impact-constrained TSMOM at \$100K scale: monthly rebalancing raises
M4 Sharpe from $+0.020$ to $+0.230$, an $11.5\times$ improvement,
primarily through reduced order-size-to-ADV ratios.
Vol-targeted sizing at this scale worsens impact outcomes by adding
leverage during low-vol periods.}

\subsection{Vol-Targeted Sizing: Implementation Details}
\label{app:vol_target_spec}

The vol-targeted variant modifies the canonical TSMOM-60 signal as
follows. On each trading day $t$ where the base strategy emits a BUY
signal, the position weight is scaled by:
\begin{equation}
  w_t = \min\!\left(\frac{\sigma^*}{\hat\sigma_t},\; L_{\max}\right)
  \label{eq:vol_target}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item $\sigma^* = 10\%$ (annualised target volatility),
  \item $\hat\sigma_t = \sigma_{20d}(r_{t-20:t-1}) \times \sqrt{252}$
    is the 20-day trailing annualised volatility computed from
    log returns, and
  \item $L_{\max} = 1.5$ is the maximum leverage multiplier (hard cap).
\end{itemize}

\noindent When $\hat\sigma_t < \sigma^*$ (low-vol regime), the
multiplier $w_t > 1$ increases position size, potentially up to
$1.5\times$ the canonical weight. This amplifies the trade value
$V_{\rm trade}$ and, because the square-root impact cost is
$\iota \propto (V_{\rm trade}/V_{\rm ADV})^{0.5}$, the convex
cost function punishes the enlarged trades disproportionately.
At \$100K portfolio scale, the ADV denominator is large enough
that the participation rate remains below $\rho = 0.05$, but the
absolute dollar impact cost rises with the squared-root of the
increased trade size.

This mechanism explains the negative M4 Sharpe ($-0.081$): the
vol-targeted variant increases trade size precisely during
low-vol, low-ADV periods where impact costs are highest per
dollar traded. Monthly rebalancing achieves the opposite effect
(fewer, less frequent trades), making it the more effective
design lever at this scale.

\section{Almgren 3/5 Power Law: Impact Model Robustness Check}
\label{app:power_law}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\citet{almgren2005direct} reject the square-root temporary-impact
model in favour of a $3/5$ power law, $\Delta P \propto \sigma\eta(Q/V)^{0.6}$,
using a large proprietary institutional dataset.
We implement both specifications---square-root ($\alpha = 0.5$, our
baseline) and Almgren's $3/5$ law ($\alpha = 0.6$)---and compare
Sharpe ratios across M0--M4 for TSMOM-60 and CSMOM-60 over the
canonical 2005--2025 period.

The two formulas are:
\begin{align}
  \iota_t^{\rm sqrt}     &= \kimp \cdot (V_{\rm trade}/V_{\rm ADV})^{0.5} \cdot 10{,}000
  \quad \text{(baseline, Lillo 2003)} \\
  \iota_t^{3/5}          &= \kimp \cdot (V_{\rm trade}/V_{\rm ADV})^{0.6} \cdot 10{,}000
  \quad \text{(Almgren et al.\ 2005)}
\end{align}
with $\kimp = 0.50$ and $\rho = 0.05$ (i.e., $Q/V \approx 0.05$).
Note that Almgren's original formula additionally normalises by daily
volatility; we apply the exponent only (without volatility normalisation)
to maintain a common $\kimp$ scale and isolate the exponent effect.

\paragraph{A counterintuitive direction.}
For $x \in (0, 1)$, $x^{0.6} < x^{0.5}$ (the higher exponent compresses
small values more strongly). At participation rate $\rho = 0.05$:
\[
  (0.05)^{0.6} \approx 0.158 \quad < \quad 0.224 \approx (0.05)^{0.5}.
\]
Therefore, at the canonical 5\% participation rate, the $3/5$ law
\emph{generates less impact cost than the square-root}, and M4 Sharpe
is \emph{better} (higher) under the $3/5$ specification.
The reversal $(x^{0.6} > x^{0.5})$ only occurs for $x > 1$, which
is outside the feasible range given $Q/V \le \rho = 0.05$.
Consequently, the main paper's square-root model is the
\emph{more conservative} (pessimistic) specification at our
calibration.

\subsection{Methodology: Standalone Ablation Backtester}
\label{app:power_law_method}

The power-law comparison uses a standalone ablation backtester rather
than the main engine to isolate the exponent effect from other engine
features. The key simplifications are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textit{Simplified execution handler:} The standalone script
    applies market-impact costs as an immediate price adjustment at
    trade time, bypassing the main engine's FIFO order queue, partial
    fill logic, and intraday settlement cycle. This removes the
    latency-dependent interactions between consecutive fills.
  \item \textit{No integer-lot rounding:} All positions are treated
    as fractional shares, eliminating the discrete lot sizes and their
    interaction residual (cf.\ Table~\ref{tab:mdd_decomp}).
  \item \textit{Single-pass portfolio construction:} Weights are
    applied directly without the main engine's sequential
    position-sizing and rebalancing passes.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent These simplifications inflate M0 Sharpe by a factor of
approximately $2.5\times$ ($1.305$ vs.\ $0.526$ for TSMOM-60;
$1.456$ vs.\ $0.583$ for CSMOM-60). The inflation is consistent
across both strategies, confirming it reflects the simplified handler
rather than a strategy-specific artifact.

\textbf{Interpretation guideline:} Because both impact models
(square-root and $3/5$ law) are evaluated within the \emph{same}
standalone framework, the M0 inflation cancels when comparing M4
Sharpes across models. The relevant quantity is the \emph{direction
and magnitude of the M4 difference} ($\Delta$ column), not the
absolute M4 levels.
