\begin{abstract}
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems span a large design space---%
sparse, dense, and hybrid retrievers; reranking; chunking strategies;
caching---yet published evaluations almost always report a single quality
number, hiding the tradeoffs that govern real deployments.
We introduce a reproducible evaluation harness that maps this design space as
Pareto frontiers jointly over Recall@$K$, end-to-end latency, and per-query
cost.
The harness explicitly separates cold-start overhead (corpus embedding and
index construction) from warm-start per-query latency, a distinction absent
from prior work but critical for serverless and autoscaling deployments.
We evaluate a fully factorial design of 36 configurations---retrieval type
$\in$ \{BM25, dense, hybrid\}, $K \in \{5, 8, 12\}$, chunk size $\in$
\{150, 250\} words, reranking on/off---on 500 queries from
HotpotQA~\citep{yang2018hotpotqa} (dev$_{\text{distractor}}$ split,
evaluated on a 2.3\,GHz Intel Core i7 CPU).
The results reveal a counterintuitive ranking reversal:
under warm-start benchmarking, dense retrieval achieves both higher Recall@12
\emph{and} lower latency (8.9\,ms) than BM25 (10.7\,ms), placing BM25
off the warm-latency Pareto frontier entirely.
Cross-encoder reranking with a $2K$ candidate pool further improves
Recall@12---Hybrid+rerank reaches 0.917 (vs.\ 0.890 no-rerank) at 64.4\,ms.
When cold-start costs are included, BM25 cold-start (133--136\,ms) is
85--108$\times$ lower than dense (11.5--14.3\,s), driven by corpus encoding
(90--92\,\% of cold-start cost, depending on chunk size).
A corpus-scale analysis over 1K--50K passages confirms that dense cold-start
grows linearly with corpus size---reaching $\approx$1.6\,minutes (96\,s) at 50K passages
while BM25 remains $<$2\,seconds---and never crosses BM25 for cold-start cost.
The harness includes an end-to-end LLM generation stage (Llama-3-8B via
Ollama); CPU inference adds approximately 5.5\,s per query, demonstrating that
generation dominates end-to-end latency by 1.9--2.8 orders of magnitude
(85--618$\times$) over retrieval---an important practical finding for
deployment planning.
Hybrid retrieval reaches the highest Recall@12 (0.890, 95\,\% Wilson CI:
0.860--0.915).
The 0.092 hybrid--BM25 gap is statistically significant ($z\!=\!4.01$,
$p\!<\!0.001$).
The 0.040 dense--BM25 gap falls below our pre-specified minimum detectable
effect ($\Delta_{\min}\!=\!0.07$) and correspondingly does not reach
significance ($z\!=\!1.64$, $p\!=\!0.10$); this study is underpowered for
differences of this magnitude ($n \geq 1{,}500$ would be needed for 80\,\%
power at $\Delta = 0.04$).
A span-level evaluation on LegalBench-RAG PrivacyQA ($n=194$) reveals a
domain-dependent reversal: dense retrieval achieves Recall@12 of 0.791 vs.\
0.282 for BM25 ($z=10.06$, $p<0.001$), showing that BM25's lexical matching
fails on paraphrastic specialized-domain text.
All code, configurations, and result data are available at
\url{https://github.com/sgupta285/Research-Projects/tree/main/rag-pareto-frontiers}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Consider two retrieval systems that report similar warm-query latency figures
in their respective papers---numbers that appear comparable but were measured
under such different conditions that neither means what it claims.
One was evaluated in a persistent process where the embedding model had already
been loaded by a previous configuration; the other used a freshly spawned
instance that paid the full corpus-encoding cost on the first query.
This incomparability is not a hypothetical.
Across the retrieval and RAG literature~\citep{lewis2020rag}, cold-start
corpus-encoding costs are routinely absorbed into warm-query figures;
in-process caches silently subsidize dense configurations during multi-sweep
evaluations; and quality is reported as a scalar rather than as one axis of a
multidimensional tradeoff.

The design space for modern retrieval pipelines is vast: inverted-index
retrieval based on BM25~\citep{robertson2009bm25},
dense bi-encoders~\citep{karpukhin2020dpr},
hybrid score fusion~\citep{cormack2009rrf,lin2022aggretriever},
cross-encoder reranking~\citep{nogueira2019passage},
chunking strategies, and semantic caching layers~\citep{bang2023gptcache}
can all be composed in many ways, each occupying a different point in the
joint space of recall, latency, and cost.
Existing benchmarks---BEIR~\citep{thakur2021beir},
MTEB~\citep{muennighoff2023mteb}---measure quality in isolation.
RAGAS~\citep{es2023ragas} measures end-to-end generation quality but ignores
efficiency.
No shared framework exists for comparing retrieval configurations across all
three dimensions on equal footing.

This paper makes five contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
  \item A reproducible RAG evaluation harness with a two-level caching
        protocol ensuring cold-start measurements are order-invariant across
        sweep executions, plus an optional LLM generation stage
        (\S\ref{sec:harness}).
  \item A fully factorial evaluation of 36 configurations on HotpotQA,
        revealing that (a) under warm-only benchmarking, dense retrieval
        dominates BM25 on both quality and latency; but (b) when cold-start
        costs are included, BM25 regains Pareto-optimal status because its
        cold-start is 85--108$\times$ lower---a ranking reversal that
        warm-only benchmarks cannot detect (\S\ref{sec:results}).
  \item A corpus-scale cold-start analysis over 1K--50K passages showing that
        dense cold-start grows linearly and exceeds BM25 by 48--76$\times$
        (corpus-size-dependent), with dense reaching $\approx$1.6\,minutes (96\,s)
        at 50K passages
        (\S\ref{sec:results_scale}).
  \item Significance tests showing the hybrid--BM25 gap is significant
        ($z=4.01$, $p<0.001$) while the dense--BM25 gap is not ($z=1.64$,
        $p=0.10$) at $n=500$; and a generation-stage integration confirming
        that CPU LLM inference ($\approx$5.5\,s/query) dominates retrieval
        latency by 1.9--2.8 orders of magnitude (\S\ref{sec:results}).
  \item A span-level evaluation on LegalBench-RAG PrivacyQA ($n=194$)
        showing that dense retrieval dramatically outperforms BM25 on
        domain-specialized legal text (Recall@12 0.791 vs.\ 0.282,
        $z=10.06$, $p<0.001$), demonstrating that the optimal retrieval
        strategy is corpus-dependent (\S\ref{sec:legalbench_results}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{RAG pipeline extensions.}
The original RAG framework~\citep{lewis2020rag} conditions an LLM reader on
top-$k$ passages from a dense retriever~\citep{karpukhin2020dpr}.
Iterative retrieval~\citep{shao2023iter}, self-reflective
generation~\citep{asai2024selfrag}, query rewriting~\citep{ma2023query},
and chain-of-thought interleaving~\citep{trivedi2023interleaving} each reopen
the latency--quality tradeoff by multiplying per-query retrieval calls in ways
rarely quantified.

\paragraph{Retrieval benchmarks.}
BEIR~\citep{thakur2021beir} provides zero-shot retrieval evaluation across many
domains; MTEB~\citep{muennighoff2023mteb} extends this to a broad embedding
suite.
Neither reports latency or cost.
RAGAS~\citep{es2023ragas} evaluates end-to-end RAG faithfulness but ignores
efficiency.
The present harness fills this gap by treating all three dimensions as
first-class outputs and producing a joint Pareto frontier.

\paragraph{Efficiency--effectiveness tradeoffs.}
\citet{hofstaetter2022efficiently} plot efficiency--effectiveness curves showing
that a distilled cross-encoder can match cross-encoder quality at bi-encoder
inference cost, motivating frontier thinking at the component level.
The present work applies this reasoning at the full-pipeline level.
\citet{kim2023full} survey the full optimisation stack for transformer inference,
highlighting the gap between theoretical and measured throughput across hardware
and software configurations.
\citet{oakes2022cold} study serverless cold-start overhead for model-serving
workloads; in practice, cold-start penalties depend strongly on whether artifact
state is preserved across requests.

\paragraph{Hybrid retrieval.}
Score-level hybrid retrieval is most commonly implemented via Reciprocal Rank
Fusion~\citep{cormack2009rrf} or convex score combination.
Aggretriever~\citep{lin2022aggretriever} aggregates dense representations for
robust retrieval.
SPLADE~\citep{formal2021splade,formal2022splade2} learns sparse expansions
bridging BM25 and dense retrieval; evaluating SPLADE in this harness is a
natural extension.

\paragraph{Dense retrieval infrastructure.}
FAISS~\citep{johnson2019faiss} provides the flat inner-product index used here.
ColBERT~\citep{khattab2020colbert} and PLAID~\citep{santhanam2022plaid} offer
late-interaction retrieval with qualitatively different latency profiles;
benchmarking them is left for future work.

\section{Evaluation Harness and Experimental Controls}
\label{sec:harness}

\subsection{Overview}

The harness comprises four sequential stages: dataset loading and corpus
construction; artifact building (encoder loading and index construction);
per-query evaluation with timing instrumentation; and result aggregation with
Pareto analysis.
Every stage is parameterized by versioned YAML configuration files, so sweeps
over the design space require no code changes.
All figures and tables in Section~\ref{sec:results} are fully reproducible from
the included configuration and data via \texttt{make figures}.

\subsection{Decomposed Timing Model and Cold-Start Accounting}
\label{sec:timing}

The central contribution of the harness is a timing model that is simultaneously
accurate and \emph{order-invariant}: the latency attributed to a configuration
does not depend on whether it was the first or fiftieth configuration evaluated
in a sweep.
Standard harnesses fail this property because Python's in-process module cache
allows an encoder loaded during configuration~$A$ to be reused silently for
configuration~$B$, reporting zero loading cost for~$B$ even though a cold
deployment would pay the full corpus-encoding cost.

The harness addresses this with a two-level canonical caching protocol.
Encoder weights are stored in a module-level dictionary
$\mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{model}}: \text{str} \to (\text{model}, \mathbb{R})$,
keyed by model name.
FAISS indices and pre-computed embeddings are stored separately, keyed by a
compound fingerprint:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{index}} =
    \mathrm{SHA\text{-}1}(\mathrm{corpus\_fp} \mathbin{\|}
    \mathrm{mode} \mathbin{\|} \mathrm{chunk\_size} \mathbin{\|}
    \mathrm{overlap} \mathbin{\|} \mathrm{model\_name}),
  \label{eq:key}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{corpus\_fp}$ is a lightweight hash of the first 200 document
titles and lengths, and $\|$ denotes string concatenation.
When a key is absent, the actual load time is measured and stored as the
\emph{canonical} cost; when present, the canonical cost is reported even though
no work was performed, ensuring that total cold-start latency
\begin{equation}
  T_{\mathrm{cold}} = T_{\mathrm{model}} + T_{\mathrm{index}} +
    T_{\mathrm{e2e,cold}}
  \label{eq:tcold}
\end{equation}
is identical regardless of sweep execution order.
Here $T_{\mathrm{model}}$ is encoder model load time,
$T_{\mathrm{index}}$ is corpus embedding plus index construction time, and
$T_{\mathrm{e2e,cold}}$ is the end-to-end latency of the first query
(which may include additional one-time costs such as deserializing cached
artifacts).
Warm-start latency $\bar{T}_{\mathrm{warm}}$ is the mean over queries
$i \geq 2$; its standard deviation $\sigma_{\mathrm{warm}}$ is also recorded.

\subsection{Pipeline Configuration Space}
\label{sec:configs}

The sweep covers a \textbf{fully factorial} design of
$3 \times 3 \times 2 \times 2 = 36$ configurations:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item Retrieval type: BM25, dense, hybrid
  \item Top-$K$: $\{5, 8, 12\}$
  \item Chunk size: $\{150, 250\}$ words (overlap fixed at 30 words for all
        configurations)
  \item Reranking: enabled / disabled
\end{itemize}
Retrieval caching is enabled for all configurations (not a sweep dimension).
Hybrid retrieval combines min-max-normalized BM25 and dense scores with mixing
weight $\alpha = 0.5$ (a standard neutral default; see \S\ref{sec:ablations}).
The dense encoder is \texttt{sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2}
\citep{reimers2019sbert}, a 22M-parameter bi-encoder widely used in production
retrieval stacks.
The BM25 index uses the \texttt{rank\_bm25} library (BM25Okapi variant).
FAISS~\citep{johnson2019faiss} \texttt{IndexFlatIP} (exact inner-product) is
used for dense retrieval.
Reranking uses \texttt{cross-encoder/ms-marco-MiniLM-L-6-v2}~\citep{nogueira2019passage},
a 6-layer BERT cross-encoder fine-tuned on MS~MARCO passage ranking.
For reranked configurations, the retriever fetches $2K$ candidates; the
cross-encoder scores all $2K$ (query, passage) pairs and returns the top $K$.
All inference is CPU-only for reproducibility.

\subsection{LLM Generation Stage}
\label{sec:generation}

The harness supports an optional end-to-end LLM generation stage invoked
after retrieval and reranking.
Retrieved passages are concatenated into a context window (up to 600
characters per passage) and submitted to the LLM as a prompted reading
comprehension task:

\begin{quote}\small\ttfamily
Answer the question based solely on the passages below.\\
Be concise (1 to 2 sentences).\\
Question: \{q\}. Passages: \{p$_1$\}\ldots\{p$_K$\}. Answer:
\end{quote}

Three backends are supported: (1)~\textbf{Ollama} (local CPU inference,
zero marginal cost, temperature 0 for determinism); (2)~\textbf{Anthropic API}
(cloud inference, billed per token, activated by setting \texttt{ANTHROPIC\_API\_KEY}); and
(3)~\textbf{mock} (returns the first 80 characters of the top passage, for
unit testing).
The pilot evaluation in Section~\ref{sec:results_gen} uses Llama-3-8B via
Ollama running on CPU.
Switching backends requires only a one-line YAML change
(\texttt{generation.backend: anthropic}).

Per-query generation metrics recorded are: token F1~\citep{yang2018hotpotqa},
exact match (EM), faithfulness (fraction of answer tokens present in the
retrieved passages), generation latency, and generation cost.
The generation cost for the Anthropic backend is computed from actual
token usage returned by the API; Ollama cost is always \$0 (local inference).

\section{Benchmark Dataset}
\label{sec:benchmarks}

\subsection{HotpotQA (Primary Evaluation)}

HotpotQA~\citep{yang2018hotpotqa} is a multi-hop question-answering dataset
requiring simultaneous retrieval of two gold passages.
We use the distractor development split, which provides ten candidate passages
per question (two gold, eight distractors), pooling all passages across
questions to form the retrieval corpus ($\approx\!5{,}000$ passages at 500
questions).

We evaluate on a random sample of 500 questions.
A power analysis following the standard two-proportion sample-size
formula~\citep{cohen1988power} confirms that $n = 500$ provides
80\,\% power to detect Recall@$K$ differences of
$\Delta \geq 0.07$ at $\alpha = 0.05$ (two-sided proportion test).
This threshold reflects a tradeoff between evaluation cost and
statistical sensitivity: differences smaller than 0.07 are unlikely
to be practically significant for deployment decisions, as they fall
within the uncertainty of retrieval systems in production.
The observed 0.040 dense--BM25 gap falls below this threshold and
is correspondingly non-significant (\S\ref{sec:results_latency});
detecting effects of this magnitude would require
$n \geq 1{,}500$ for 80\,\% power.
We note that many published retrieval comparisons at similar sample
sizes ($n = 500$--$1{,}000$) may be similarly underpowered,
potentially mistaking null results for evidence of equivalence
rather than recognizing insufficient power.
Because both gold passages must appear in the top-$K$ for full credit,
Recall@$K$ rewards broad, diverse coverage.

\subsection{LegalBench-RAG (Supported Protocol)}
\label{sec:legalbench}

LegalBench-RAG~\citep{pipitone2024legalbenchrag} provides question-answer pairs
grounded in statutory and case-law corpora, annotated at the character-span
level.
The harness supports LegalBench-RAG evaluation via a character-overlap
recall/precision protocol (\texttt{metrics.py::retrieval\_span\_%
recall\_precision}).
A retrieved chunk counts as a true positive only if its character range overlaps
with the annotated supporting span---a stricter criterion than document-level
recall.
The corpus is publicly available under CC-BY from the benchmark
repository (\url{https://github.com/zeroentropy-ai/legalbenchrag}).
Full results are reported in Section~\ref{sec:legalbench_results}; the
span-level evaluation protocol informed several harness design choices
(\texttt{char\_start}/\texttt{char\_end} fields in \texttt{Chunk} objects).

\section{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

\subsection{Retrieval Quality}

Retrieval Recall@$K$ on HotpotQA is computed at the document-title level:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Recall@}K = \frac{1}{|Q|} \sum_{q \in Q}
    \frac{|\mathcal{R}_q^K \cap \mathcal{G}_q|}{|\mathcal{G}_q|},
  \label{eq:recall}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{R}_q^K$ is the set of retrieved titles in the top~$K$ and
$\mathcal{G}_q$ is the gold title set.
Since $K$ varies across configurations ($K \in \{5, 8, 12\}$), a configuration
with larger $K$ has a mechanical recall advantage independent of retriever
quality.
All Pareto frontier analyses and cross-retriever comparisons in
Section~\ref{sec:results} are conducted at \textbf{fixed $K = 12$}; results
for smaller $K$ are shown in the recall-vs-$K$ ablation
(Section~\ref{sec:ablations}, Figure~\ref{fig:recall_k}).

\paragraph{Confidence intervals.}
All Recall@$K$ estimates are reported with 95\,\% Wilson score confidence
intervals~\citep{wilson1927probable}, which have better coverage properties
than the normal approximation for proportions near the boundaries and for
moderate sample sizes~\citep{agresti1998approximate}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CI}_{95} = \frac{\hat{p} + z^2/(2n) \pm z\sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})/n
  + z^2/(4n^2)}}{1 + z^2/n},
  \quad z = 1.96.
  \label{eq:wilson}
\end{equation}
For $\hat{p} = 0.84$ and $n = 500$ this gives a half-width of approximately
$\pm 0.032$.

\paragraph{Statistical testing.}
Pairwise comparisons use two-sided two-proportion $z$-tests~\citep{altman1991practical},
reporting the $z$-score for the difference of two independent proportions
and the corresponding two-sided $p$-value.

\subsection{Latency}

Four latency quantities are reported per configuration:
$T_{\mathrm{model}}$ (encoder load time),
$T_{\mathrm{index}}$ (corpus embedding plus index construction),
$\bar{T}_{\mathrm{warm}} \pm \sigma_{\mathrm{warm}}$ (mean $\pm$ std warm-start
latency over queries $i \geq 2$), and $T_{\mathrm{cold}}$
(Equation~\eqref{eq:tcold}).

\subsection{Cost}

Per-query marginal inference cost tracks API spend:
\begin{equation}
  C_q = C_{\mathrm{gen}},
  \label{eq:cost}
\end{equation}
where $C_{\mathrm{gen}} = (n_{\mathrm{in}}/1{,}000) \times p_{\mathrm{in}} +
(n_{\mathrm{out}}/1{,}000) \times p_{\mathrm{out}}$ (LLM generation cost, zero
if generation is disabled or uses a local backend such as Ollama).
The cross-encoder reranker is a local model; its cost is compute latency
overhead (reported separately in Section~\ref{sec:results_cost}), not API spend.
\textbf{Scope note:} $C_q$ tracks \emph{per-query API costs only}.
Corpus embedding (one-time offline cost), storage, egress, and compute
infrastructure costs are excluded.

\subsection{Generation Quality}
\label{sec:gen_metrics}

When the generation stage is active, three metrics are computed per query:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Token F1:} overlap between normalized prediction and gold
        answer token bags.
        Used by the SQuAD/HotpotQA community; reported but \emph{not} a primary
        metric here, because generative LLMs produce explanatory answers that
        are penalized by extractive gold labels.
  \item \textbf{Exact Match (EM):} 1 if normalized prediction equals
        normalized gold answer, 0 otherwise.
  \item \textbf{Faithfulness:} fraction of non-trivial answer tokens
        (excluding stop words) present in at least one retrieved passage.
        This measures groundedness independently of gold labels.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Quality vs.\ Warm-Start Latency (Fixed $K=12$)}
\label{sec:results_latency}

Figure~\ref{fig:pareto_latency} shows the Pareto frontier in the
quality--warm-latency plane at fixed $K = 12$.
The finding is counterintuitive relative to common assumptions:
dense configurations are \emph{both} faster and more accurate than BM25 in the
warm-only view, placing BM25 entirely off the warm Pareto frontier.

Dense retrieval achieves 8.9\,ms warm latency because FAISS flat
inner-product search over $\approx\!5{,}000$ passages is a single matrix
multiplication, a highly optimized BLAS operation even on CPU.
BM25 requires iterating over inverted lists for each query term, yielding
10.7\,ms at this corpus scale.%
\footnote{At production scale ($10^6$--$10^7$ passages),
FAISS~\citep{johnson2019faiss} flat inner-product search
($O(d \cdot n)$ complexity, $d = 384$) becomes slower than BM25's inverted-index
structure ($O(m \cdot d_{\mathrm{avg}})$ for $m$ query terms).
The reversal here is specific to the $\approx\!5{,}000$-passage corpus where
dense search remains faster empirically.}

The warm Pareto frontier at $K=12$ contains three configurations
(Table~\ref{tab:pareto}):
hybrid+rerank (chunk\,=\,250, Recall@12\,=\,0.917, 64.4\,ms),
hybrid no-rerank (chunk\,=\,250, Recall@12\,=\,0.890, 22.3\,ms), and
dense no-rerank (chunk\,=\,250, Recall@12\,=\,0.838, 8.9\,ms).

\paragraph{Statistical comparisons.}
The hybrid--BM25 recall gap (0.890 vs.\ 0.798, $\Delta = 0.092$) is
statistically significant ($z = 4.01$, $p < 0.001$, two-sided two-proportion
$z$-test) and exceeds our pre-specified minimum detectable effect
($\Delta_{\min} = 0.07$), providing strong evidence of a genuine quality
advantage.

The dense--BM25 recall gap (0.838 vs.\ 0.798, $\Delta = 0.040$) does not
reach statistical significance ($z = 1.64$, $p = 0.10$).
Critically, this gap falls below our minimum detectable effect size (0.07),
meaning this study was not designed to distinguish differences of this
magnitude---the null result is expected given the power constraints, not
surprising.
The 95\,\% Wilson CIs for dense [0.803, 0.868] and BM25 [0.760, 0.830]
overlap substantially, reinforcing that the observed difference is within
sampling variability for $n = 500$.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
We cannot conclude that dense and BM25 have equivalent quality---only that
our study lacks power to detect differences of $\Delta = 0.04$.
Detecting this effect size would require $n \geq 1{,}500$ for 80\,\% power.
Many published retrieval comparisons at $n = 500$--$1{,}000$ may face
identical power limitations, mistaking non-significant results for evidence
of equivalence when in fact the study simply lacks sensitivity to detect
small-to-moderate effects.
For practitioners, the actionable finding is the warm-latency ordering: dense
achieves numerically higher recall (point estimate $+0.040$) at lower latency
(8.9\,ms vs.\ 10.7\,ms), though the quality advantage is not statistically
confirmed at this sample size.

The gap between the two cluster-centers in the warm-latency plot
(Figure~\ref{fig:pareto_latency}) is an artifact of the discrete configuration
space evaluated, not an architectural constant.

\subsection{Quality vs.\ Reranking Overhead}
\label{sec:results_cost}

Figure~\ref{fig:pareto_cost} shows Recall@$K$ vs.\ the mean per-query
reranking latency overhead (ms) for configurations at $K=12$.
Reranked configurations (triangles) retrieve $2K=24$ candidates, then score
all 24 (query, passage) pairs with the
\texttt{cross-encoder/ms-marco-MiniLM-L-6-v2} cross-encoder, returning the
top $K=12$, adding approximately 42--47\,ms per query on CPU.

\paragraph{2K pool expansion improves Recall@K.}
Cross-encoder reranking with a $2K$ candidate pool \emph{does} improve
Recall@12 compared to retrieval-only at $K=12$:
BM25+rerank reaches 0.846 (vs.\ 0.797 no-rerank, $+0.049$);
Dense+rerank reaches 0.881 (vs.\ 0.838, $+0.043$);
Hybrid+rerank reaches the highest overall Recall@12 of 0.917
(vs.\ 0.890, $+0.027$; 95\,\% CI: 0.890--0.938).
The gain comes from the expanded candidate pool: some gold passages rank
outside the top $K$ in the bi-encoder but are surfaced in positions $K+1$
to $2K$, then correctly promoted by the cross-encoder.

\paragraph{Latency--quality tradeoff.}
The Pareto-optimal configuration at $K=12$ is therefore Hybrid+rerank
(Recall=0.917, warm=64.4\,ms) for quality-optimized deployments, or
Dense no-rerank (Recall=0.838, warm=8.9\,ms) for latency-optimized
deployments, with no-rerank Hybrid (0.890, 22.3\,ms) as an intermediate option.
The cross-encoder is a local model (zero API cost); all reranking cost is
CPU compute time.

\subsection{Cold-Start vs.\ Warm-Start Latency}
\label{sec:results_cold}

Figure~\ref{fig:cold_warm} reveals the ranking reversal that motivates this
paper.
BM25 cold-start (133--136\,ms) requires only building an inverted index from
the tokenized corpus.
Dense cold-start (11.5--14.3\,s) comprises two steps:
(1)~\textbf{Model loading}: $T_{\mathrm{model}} \approx 1{,}210$\,ms
($\approx\!8\%$ of total cold-start);
(2)~\textbf{Corpus embedding}: $T_{\mathrm{index}} \approx 10.3$--$13.1$\,s
($\approx\!92\%$).
The dominant cost is corpus encoding, not model loading.
This has an important practical implication: the primary mitigation for
dense retrieval in serverless settings is not faster model loading but
\emph{offline pre-computation and persistence of the encoded corpus}.

The chunk-size effect is visible: chunk\,=\,150 produces more chunks than
chunk\,=\,250 (step sizes 120 vs.\ 220 words after overlap), adding
approximately 2.8\,s to index build time and yielding a total cold-start
of 14.3\,s vs.\ 11.5\,s.

In a serverless deployment where instance state is not preserved between
requests, dense retrieval pays the full cold-start penalty ($\approx\!11.5$--$14.3$\,s)
on every cold request, making it 85--108$\times$ more expensive in cold-start
terms than BM25.
In a persistent warm deployment, dense retrieval's 8.9\,ms warm latency
dominates BM25 (10.7\,ms) on both quality and latency.

\paragraph{Mixing weight $\alpha$.}
The hybrid retriever uses $\alpha = 0.5$ (equal weight to BM25 and dense scores

The hybrid advantage over pure dense (0.890 vs.\ 0.838, $\Delta = 0.052$,

non-redundant information even when combined with semantic embeddings.
Ablating $\alpha \in \{0.3, 0.5, 0.7\}$ is left for future work.
Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF)~\citep{cormack2009rrf} provides a parameter-free
alternative to score combination; direct comparison of RRF vs.\ convex mixing
would clarify whether the $\alpha$ hyperparameter is worth tuning.

\subsection{End-to-End Generation Stage}
\label{sec:results_gen}

The harness supports an optional LLM generation stage (\S\ref{sec:generation})
that can be enabled with a single YAML parameter change.
To validate pipeline integration, we ran a small technical pilot
($n = 5$ HotpotQA queries) using Llama-3-8B via Ollama on CPU
(temperature~0).
The sample size ($n=5$) is not powered for statistical inference about
retrieval-conditional generation quality; full details are provided in
Appendix~\ref{app:generation_pilot}.

\paragraph{Key finding: generation dominates retrieval latency.}
CPU inference adds approximately 5.5\,s per query, dwarfing retrieval
latency by 1.9--2.8 orders of magnitude (85--618$\times$: 5.5\,s
generation vs.\ 8.9--64.4\,ms retrieval).
For on-device CPU inference, retrieval type choice is nearly irrelevant
for end-to-end latency.
On GPU (A100), the same model generates in $\approx\!50$\,ms, restoring
retrieval as the latency bottleneck.
For API-based LLMs (e.g., \texttt{claude-haiku-4-5-20251001}), generation
takes $\approx\!200$--$600$\,ms/query---comparable in magnitude to retrieval
for small corpora.

The pilot also confirmed that Token F1 and Exact Match are systematically
low for generative output (HotpotQA golds are short extractive phrases
while LLMs produce explanatory sentences), demonstrating that proper
evaluation of generative RAG requires generation-aware metrics (ROUGE-L,
BERTScore, or LLM-as-judge), not extractive QA metrics.
Full generation evaluation at $n \geq 200$ with GPU inference and proper
metrics is left for future work.

\subsection{Corpus-Scale Cold-Start Analysis}
\label{sec:results_scale}

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{../outputs/figures/fig_cold_scale.png}
\caption{Cold-start latency vs.\ corpus size (log--log scale) for BM25 and
dense retrieval over 5 timing repeats each (error bars = $\pm 1$ std).
Dense cold-start grows linearly with corpus size (slope $\approx 1$ in
log--log), reaching $\approx$1.6\,minutes (96\,s) at 50K passages.
BM25 cold-start also grows linearly but 48--75$\times$ slower (corpus-size
dependent), remaining $<$2.1\,s at 50K passages.
The ordering never crosses: BM25 cold-start is always faster than dense for
any corpus size evaluated.}
\label{fig:cold_scale}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:cold_scale} extends the cold-start analysis from the primary
5K-passage corpus to scales representative of small and medium production
deployments.
Because the base HotpotQA corpus contains $\approx\!5{,}000$ unique passages,
corpora above this size are constructed by padding: base passages are repeated
with minor text perturbations (a numeric suffix) so that each copy has a
distinct token sequence and no encoder or index cache can short-circuit the
build.  This correctly stresses the dominant cold-start cost---corpus
encoding---while keeping the experiment self-contained.

\smallskip\noindent
\textit{What generalizes:}
Absolute cold-start times (seconds) and linear scaling behaviour
generalize to real production corpora because encoding cost is
determined by volume (number of chunks), not semantic diversity.
\textit{What may not generalize:}
Dense retrieval \emph{quality} (Recall@$K$) on synthetic corpora may
differ from real diverse corpora because semantic embeddings are
sensitive to lexical variety.
Validation on real large-scale corpora (Wikipedia, MS~MARCO full corpus)
is needed before extrapolating quality conclusions to production scale;
the cold-start timing conclusions are robust.

\paragraph{Linear scaling.}
Dense cold-start time is dominated by corpus encoding
($\approx\!90\%$ of total), which scales as
$T_{\mathrm{encode}} = n_{\mathrm{chunks}} \cdot t_{\mathrm{encode}}$
where $t_{\mathrm{encode}} \approx 1.9$\,ms/chunk on this CPU.
At 50K passages ($\approx\!51{,}000$ chunks), dense cold-start reaches
$96 \pm 1$\,s ($\approx\!1.6$\,min, mean $\pm$ std over 5 repeats),
whereas BM25 remains $2.0 \pm 0.1$\,s.
The ratio (dense/BM25) varies from $\approx\!75\times$ at 1K passages to
$\approx\!48\times$ at 50K, reflecting the fixed model-load cost
($\approx\!1{,}275$\,ms) which is amortized at larger corpus sizes.

\paragraph{No crossover.}
The cold-start ordering (BM25 $<$ dense) is stable at every corpus size
tested (1K, 2.5K, 5K, 10K, 25K, 50K passages).
The commonly cited expectation that FAISS flat search becomes the bottleneck
at large scale applies to \emph{warm-query} latency
(Section~\ref{sec:results_latency}, footnote),
not to cold-start: indexing cost always grows with corpus size.

\paragraph{Practical breakpoints.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{BM25}: acceptable cold start ($<$2\,s) at 50K passages;
        likely acceptable ($<$10\,s) up to $\approx\!200$K passages.
  \item \textbf{Dense without pre-computation}: unacceptable for serverless
        at any scale studied ($\approx\!3.4$\,s at 1K passages, 96\,s at 50K).
  \item \textbf{Dense with pre-computed index}: reduces cold start to
        $T_{\mathrm{model}} + T_{\mathrm{deser}} \approx 2$\,s,
        competitive with BM25 at large scale.
\end{itemize}
The dominant mitigation for dense serverless is therefore \emph{offline
index pre-computation and object-storage persistence}, not faster inference.

\subsection{LegalBench-RAG: Span-Level Retrieval Evaluation}
\label{sec:legalbench_results}

\input{../outputs/tables/legalbenchrag_results.tex}

Table~\ref{tab:legalbenchrag} reports span-level retrieval results on the
LegalBench-RAG PrivacyQA benchmark ($n=194$ queries across seven privacy-policy
documents, 127 chunks at chunk\,=\,250 words, $K=12$, no reranking).
A retrieved chunk is a true positive only if its character range overlaps with
the annotated supporting span---a stricter evaluation than document-title recall.

\paragraph{Domain reversal: dense retrieval dominates on specialized legal text.}
The ranking observed on HotpotQA (hybrid\,$>$\,dense\,$>$\,BM25) reverses on
LegalBench-RAG: dense retrieval achieves the highest span-level Recall@12
(0.791, 95\,\% CI: 0.729--0.842), followed by hybrid (0.715,
CI: 0.648--0.774), while BM25 is dramatically weaker (0.282,
CI: 0.223--0.349).

The Dense--BM25 gap (0.509) is large and highly significant
($z=10.06$, $p<0.001$); the Hybrid--BM25 gap (0.433) is similarly significant
($z=8.53$, $p<0.001$).
The Dense--Hybrid gap (0.076) does not reach significance at $\alpha=0.05$
($z=1.74$, $p=0.082$), though the direction favors dense.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
Privacy policy text is highly paraphrastic and uses specialized legal
vocabulary with many near-synonyms.
BM25 requires lexical overlap between query and passage; when a question asks
about ``data retention'' and the policy discusses ``how long we keep your
information,'' BM25 misses the match.
Dense bi-encoders capture semantic equivalence across such paraphrases,
explaining the 0.509 gap.
HotpotQA, by contrast, contains factoid questions with named entities that
appear verbatim in gold passages---conditions that favor BM25's lexical
matching.

\paragraph{Practical implication.}
For domain-specialized corpora (legal, medical, policy documents) with
paraphrastic terminology, dense retrieval should be preferred even when
cold-start cost is a concern, because BM25 recall may be unacceptably low.
The deployment choice between BM25 (lower cold-start) and dense (higher recall)
is therefore corpus-dependent; the present harness enables this measurement
directly.

\section{Practitioner Guidelines}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Tiered deployment framework.}
The Pareto analysis supports four deployment tiers:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=0pt,topsep=2pt,itemsep=4pt]
  \item[Quality-max service ($\bar{T}_{\mathrm{warm}} \leq 70$\,ms):]
    Hybrid+rerank, chunk\,=\,250, $K=12$;
    Recall@12\,=\,0.917, warm\,=\,64.4\,ms.
    Model, index, and cross-encoder loaded once at startup;
    not suitable for serverless.

  \item[Quality-optimized persistent service
    ($\bar{T}_{\mathrm{warm}} \leq 25$\,ms):]
    Hybrid no-rerank, chunk=250, $K=12$; Recall@12 = 0.890, warm = 22.3\,ms.
    Avoids cross-encoder overhead while retaining hybrid recall advantage.

  \item[Latency-optimized persistent service ($\bar{T}_{\mathrm{warm}} \leq
    10$\,ms):] Dense no-rerank, chunk=250, $K=12$; Recall@12 = 0.838, warm = 8.9\,ms.
    The 0.040 dense--BM25 recall gap is not significant at $n=500$ (falls
    below $\Delta_{\min} = 0.07$);
    deploying BM25 instead imposes negligible measurable quality cost while
    matching warm latency---though at larger sample sizes or corpora the dense
    advantage may become detectable.

  \item[Serverless / cold-start-constrained deployment:] BM25 (chunk\,=\,250,
    $K=12$), Recall@12\,=\,0.797, cold\,=\,135\,ms.
    Alternatively: dense with pre-computed corpus embeddings persisted to object
    storage reduces cold penalty to model load ($\approx\!1{,}210$\,ms) plus
    FAISS deserialization.
\end{description}

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Corpus scale and diversity.}
The corpus-scale cold-start analysis (\S\ref{sec:results_scale}) uses
synthetic corpus construction: base HotpotQA passages are duplicated with
numeric suffixes to stress encoding costs while keeping the experiment
self-contained.
Absolute cold-start times and the linear scaling behaviour generalize to real
production corpora of comparable size because encoding cost depends on volume,
not semantic content.
However, dense retrieval \emph{quality} on a synthetic 50K-passage corpus may
differ from 50K unique Wikipedia articles due to lower lexical diversity.
Extending to $10^6$--$10^7$ passages and to approximate nearest-neighbour
indices (HNSW, IVF) would complete the scaling picture.

\paragraph{Single encoder model.}
All dense retrieval results use \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} (22M parameters,
384-dimensional embeddings).
Larger models (E5-large~\citep{wang2024text}: 335M parameters;
BGE-M3~\citep{chen2024bge}: multilingual, multi-granularity) would shift the
quality--latency tradeoff.
Cold-start cost scales with encoding time per chunk; the Pareto positions
reported here are specific to \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2}.

\paragraph{CPU-only evaluation.}
All timing measurements are CPU-only (Intel Core i7-1068NG7).
GPU acceleration shifts latency by 10--100$\times$ depending on batch size and
hardware.
However, the \emph{relative} orderings (dense~$<$~BM25 for warm latency,
BM25~$<$~dense for cold-start) and the cold-start ratio (85--108$\times$) are
qualitatively robust because they are dominated by encoding volume, not
arithmetic throughput.
Absolute millisecond values should not be extrapolated to GPU deployments.

\paragraph{Statistical power.}
Sample size ($n=500$) provides 80\,\% power to detect Recall@$K$ differences of
$\Delta \geq 0.07$.
The observed dense--BM25 gap ($\Delta = 0.040$) falls below this threshold and
is correspondingly non-significant.
Detecting effects of this magnitude would require $n \geq 1{,}500$.
Many published retrieval comparisons at $n = 500$--$1{,}000$ face identical
power limitations.

\paragraph{Generation evaluation.}
The generation stage pilot ($n=5$, Appendix~\ref{app:generation_pilot})
validates pipeline integration but is not powered for statistical inference
about retrieval-conditional generation quality.
Full generation evaluation requires $n \geq 200$ with GPU inference and
generation-aware metrics (ROUGE-L, BERTScore, LLM-as-judge).
Token F1 and Exact Match are inappropriate for generative output.

\paragraph{LegalBench-RAG coverage.}
The LegalBench-RAG evaluation covers only the PrivacyQA sub-benchmark (7
privacy-policy documents, 194 queries).
The strong dense advantage observed here may not generalize to all legal
sub-domains; CUAD, MAUD, and ContractNLI benchmarks are available for future
ablations.

\paragraph{Unexplored design dimensions.}
Several RAG design choices were fixed without ablation:
(1)~chunk overlap (30 words): affects chunk count and coverage;
(2)~hybrid mixing weight $\alpha$ (0.5): could be optimized per-domain;
(3)~RRF vs.\ convex score combination: not compared;
(4)~approximate nearest-neighbour indices (HNSW, IVF): would reduce warm
latency at cost of recall;
(5)~cross-encoder candidate pool size ($2K$): larger pools ($4K$, $8K$) could
improve recall at higher CPU cost.
Systematic ablation of these dimensions is left for future work.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have shown that retrieval evaluation without cold-start accounting produces
rankings that can reverse under realistic deployment conditions.
The reversal is concrete and large: in the warm-latency view, dense retrieval
dominates BM25 on both quality and speed; in the cold-latency view, BM25
dominates because dense cold-start is 85--108$\times$ higher, driven by
corpus encoding (90--92\,\% of total cold-start), not model loading.

Five findings are of practical value.
First, the dense--BM25 recall gap at $K=12$ ($\Delta = 0.040$) falls below our
pre-specified minimum detectable effect (0.07) and correspondingly does not
reach significance at $n=500$ ($z=1.64$, $p=0.10$).
This is not evidence that dense and BM25 are equivalent---rather, it
demonstrates that distinguishing small quality differences ($\Delta < 0.07$)
requires larger sample sizes ($n \geq 1{,}500$ for 80\,\% power at
$\Delta = 0.04$).
Practitioners should not interpret published non-significant comparisons as
proof of equivalence; the warm-latency ordering (dense: 8.9\,ms, higher
point-estimate recall; BM25: 10.7\,ms) may matter more than the unconfirmed
quality gap.
Second, offline pre-computation of dense corpus embeddings is the most
effective cold-start mitigation for serverless deployments, reducing
cold-start from the full corpus-encoding time to model load plus FAISS
deserialization ($\approx$2\,s).
Third, the corpus-scale analysis over 1K--50K passages (5 timing repeats
each) shows that dense cold-start exceeds BM25 by 48--76$\times$ across all
sizes tested, with no crossover.
Fourth, cross-encoder reranking with a $2K$ candidate pool (retrieve $2K$,
cross-encode, return top $K$) adds 42--47\,ms on CPU and \emph{does} improve
Recall@$K$: the Hybrid+rerank configuration achieves the highest overall
Recall@12 of 0.917, up from 0.890 without reranking ($+0.027$).
The gain comes from surfacing gold passages that fall outside the top-$K$
in the bi-encoder but within the top-$2K$.
Fifth, on the LegalBench-RAG PrivacyQA benchmark, dense retrieval
substantially outperforms BM25 at the span level (Recall@12 0.791 vs.\ 0.282,
$z=10.06$, $p<0.001$), demonstrating that the optimal retrieval strategy is
corpus-dependent: BM25 is competitive on factoid multi-hop QA but fails badly
on paraphrastic specialized-domain text.

The Pareto frontier framework provides a principled basis for these
recommendations that scalar benchmarks cannot.
Extending this framework to larger corpora, GPU inference, and full end-to-end
RAG evaluation---and adopting it as a reporting standard---would give the field
the measurement discipline needed to make deployment decisions on measured
grounds.

\section{Generation Stage Technical Validation ($n = 5$)}
\label{app:generation_pilot}

This appendix documents a small-scale validation ($n = 5$ HotpotQA queries)
of the end-to-end LLM generation pipeline.
The purpose is to confirm integration correctness and measure generation
latency---\textbf{not} to perform statistical inference about
retrieval-conditional generation quality.
The sample size is deliberately minimal because the generation stage is fully
modular: practitioners can enable or disable it via a single YAML parameter
(\texttt{generation.backend: ollama}), and the core retrieval evaluation
($n = 500$) is independent of generation stage choices.

\paragraph{Configuration.}
LLM: Llama-3-8B via Ollama (local CPU inference, zero API cost).
Temperature: 0 (deterministic).
Prompt template:
\begin{quote}\small\ttfamily
Answer the question based solely on the passages below.
Be concise (1 to 2 sentences).
Question: \{q\}. Passages: \{p$_1$\}\ldots\{p$_K$\}. Answer:
\end{quote}
Context window: up to 600 characters per passage ($K$ passages concatenated).

\paragraph{Generation latency dominance.}
CPU inference with Llama-3-8B adds approximately 5.5\,s per query.
This dwarfs retrieval latency by 1.9--2.8 orders of magnitude:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item Dense (no-rerank): 5{,}500\,ms\,/\,8.9\,ms = 618$\times$
        ($10^{2.79}$)
  \item Hybrid (rerank): 5{,}500\,ms\,/\,64.4\,ms = 85$\times$
        ($10^{1.93}$)
\end{itemize}
\textit{Implication:} For on-device CPU inference, retrieval type choice is
nearly irrelevant for end-to-end latency---generation dominates.
On GPU (A100), the same model generates in $\approx$50\,ms, restoring
retrieval as the bottleneck.
For API-based LLMs (e.g., \texttt{claude-haiku-4-5-20251001}), generation takes
200--600\,ms/query including network round-trip, making retrieval latency
comparable in magnitude.

\paragraph{Token F1 and EM misalignment.}
HotpotQA gold answers are short extractive phrases (median 1.8 tokens), while
Llama-3 produces explanatory sentences.
Token F1 and EM are therefore systematically low---not as a model quality
signal but as a measurement artifact.
Proper evaluation of generative RAG requires generation-aware metrics
(ROUGE-L, BERTScore, or LLM-as-judge).

\paragraph{Faithfulness.}
All $n = 5$ generated answers were fully grounded in the retrieved passages
(faithfulness\,=\,1.0), confirming that the prompt template is effective at
preventing hallucination in this limited test.

\paragraph{Limitations of this pilot.}
$n = 5$ is not powered for statistical inference about retrieval-conditional
generation quality (e.g., ``Does hybrid retrieval produce more faithful answers
than BM25?'').
Such questions require $n \geq 200$ with properly paired retrieval
configurations.
CPU-only inference latency (5.5\,s/query) does not extrapolate to GPU or
cloud API deployments.
Token F1/EM are inappropriate metrics for generative output.
